\section{Introduction}
This thesis explores decentralized live video streaming using \ac{P2P} networking. The primary objective is to design and implement a functional prototype and evaluate its performance and stability under controlled conditions. The motivation is to address known limitations of centralized delivery models, including infrastructure cost concentration, operational single points of failure, and scaling constraints.

Most production streaming systems currently rely on centralized origins and \ac{CDN}s to deliver media. Although this model provides strong operational control and broad compatibility, provider-side outages demonstrate that central dependencies can still impact service availability at significant scale.\cite{ServiceHealthNov,peroniEndtoEndPipelinePerspective2025}

The thesis therefore examines whether \ac{P2P} transport can provide a practical complement for live distribution. It evaluates both expected benefits (resource sharing, resilience, and potential cost reduction)\cite{xuAnalysisCDNP2P2006,meskovicContentDeliveryArchitectures2012} and expected trade-offs (latency sensitivity, quality instability under churn, and security/governance requirements).\cite{SecurityConsiderations}

The scope is live video streaming with emphasis on the transport layer.

\section{Background}
\subsection{Peer-to-Peer}

Peer-to-peer (
\ac{P2P}) networking is communication model where participating nodes (peers) can directly exchange data and services without relying exclusively on a permanently available central server. In practical architecture discussions, \ac{P2P} systems are characterized by direct resource sharing between autonomous participants and reduced reliance on centralized delivery components.\cite{xuAnalysisCDNP2P2006}

From architecture perspective, \ac{P2P} systems are typically described as either pure or hybrid.\cite{meskovicContentDeliveryArchitectures2012} In pure \ac{P2P}, peers provide both control and data-plane functions in decentralized manner. In hybrid \ac{P2P}, selected centralized components are preserved for tasks such as discovery, bootstrap, or coordination, while most payload distribution is delegated to peers. This classification is important in practice because many deployed systems use hybrid designs to combine operational control with horizontal scalability.

The main advantages of \ac{P2P} include improved scalability through aggregate upload capacity of many peers, reduced dependency on single infrastructure provider, and potential cost reduction for large-scale content distribution.\cite{xuAnalysisCDNP2P2006,meskovicContentDeliveryArchitectures2012} However, these benefits are accompanied by trade-offs: peer churn, asymmetric residential connectivity, heterogeneous device capabilities, and more complex trust and abuse resistance mechanisms compared to centralized delivery.\cite{SecurityConsiderations}

For video distribution, \ac{P2P} is commonly combined with \ac{CDN} infrastructure in hybrid designs.\cite{meskovicContentDeliveryArchitectures2012} Literature indicates this approach can reduce provider bandwidth costs while supporting service continuity through \ac{CDN} fallback when peer capacity is insufficient.\cite{xuAnalysisCDNP2P2006} This hybrid approach is also reflected in modern implementations and frameworks, where transport, peer discovery, and secure communication primitives are exposed by reusable protocol stacks (for example libp2p) and browser-oriented distribution libraries.\cite{Libp2pIPFSDocs,NovageP2pmedialoader2025}

\subsection{Video Streaming}
Video streaming is a method of delivering video data over a network as a continuous flow, allowing playback without downloading the entire file in advance.\cite{apostolopoulosVideoStreamingConcepts,peroniEndtoEndPipelinePerspective2025}

The term covers both "on-demand" services (pre-recorded content fetched by the client) and "live" broadcasts where the stream is generated and consumed simultaneously. In practice the distinction blurs: on-demand systems still deliver chunks progressively, and live systems may buffer a few seconds of content to tolerate network variability.\cite{peroniEndtoEndPipelinePerspective2025} In current large-scale deployments, HTTP-based delivery (for example HLS and MPEG-DASH) is widely used because it aligns with existing web and CDN infrastructure.\cite{pantos_http_2017,noauthor_standards_nodate}

Streaming differs from simple progressive download because the receiver decodes and presents frames as they arrive; in adaptive systems, the delivered bitrate is adjusted according to network conditions (see \S\ref{sec:abr}).\cite{apostolopoulosVideoStreamingConcepts,peroniEndtoEndPipelinePerspective2025}

\subsubsection{Adaptive Bitrate Streaming}
\label{sec:abr}
\ac{ABR} streaming is the mechanism by which a player dynamically switches between multiple encoded representations of a video according to available bandwidth, buffer occupancy, and device capabilities. Without \ac{ABR}, a single high-quality stream stalls whenever the network cannot sustain the selected rate, while a low-bitrate stream underuses available capacity on better connections. \ac{ABR} logic is typically implemented client-side and depends on manifests listing available representations and segment locations. Early classes of \ac{ABR} algorithms are commonly described as rate-based, buffer-based, or hybrid, while recent work also studies learning-based optimization using network traces and \ac{QoE} data.\cite{peroniEndtoEndPipelinePerspective2025}

\subsubsection{Streaming Protocols and Formats}
Video streaming is not a single protocol but a family of delivery mechanisms. In mainstream deployments, HLS and MPEG-DASH dominate HTTP-based distribution, while RTSP and WebRTC are used in real-time and interactive scenarios.\cite{pantos_http_2017,noauthor_standards_nodate,schulzrinne_real-time_2016,noauthor_webrtc_nodate,peroniEndtoEndPipelinePerspective2025}

These protocol families impose different transport expectations. HTTP-based systems inherit in-order behavior from TCP or QUIC, whereas \ac{RTP}/UDP-style delivery exposes packet loss, jitter, and reordering more directly at the application boundary.\cite{pantos_http_2017,schulzrinne_rtp_2003} This distinction is central when evaluating \ac{P2P} transport for live media.


\subsubsection{Video Streaming Pipeline}
Peroni et al. describe the end-to-end streaming pipeline as a sequence of ingestion, processing, and distribution stages, where video is captured and encoded, transformed on the media server, and then delivered to the viewer through a player that adapts to network conditions and device capabilities \cite{peroniEndtoEndPipelinePerspective2025}.

In the context of this thesis, the most relevant part of that pipeline is the distribution stage, specifically the transport layer between producer and consumers. \ac{P2P} mechanisms are evaluated as alternatives to centralized distribution in this stage. Encoding and decoding aspects are also addressed because practical transport viability depends on media packetization format, decoder tolerance, and buffering behavior.

Modern distribution pipelines also generate manifests (playlists) and implement adaptive bitrate (ABR) logic in the player. The player then selects among multiple encoded representations based on observed throughput and buffer state (see \S\ref{sec:abr}).\cite{peroniEndtoEndPipelinePerspective2025} Manifest protocols such as HLS and MPEG-DASH are transported over HTTP, on top of TCP or QUIC. Any P2P replacement therefore needs to interoperate with or replicate these behaviours.\cite{pantos_http_2017,noauthor_standards_nodate}

According to \cite{apostolopoulosVideoStreamingConcepts}:
\begin{quote}
The basic idea of video streaming is to split the video
into parts, transmit these parts in succession, and enable the receiver to
decode and playback the video as these parts are received, without having to
wait for the entire video to be delivered.
\end{quote}

From a transport perspective, streaming can be represented by three logical roles:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Producer --- generates the media stream.
    \item Transport layer --- delivers stream data to one or more consumers.
    \item Consumer --- receives and renders the stream.
\end{itemize}
These roles are used later to compare transport implementations without changing surrounding media logic.\cite{apostolopoulosVideoStreamingConcepts}

\subsubsection{Live video streaming}
Live video streaming refers to real-time capture and transmission, where media is produced and consumed continuously rather than served as a completed file.

In large-scale deployments, \ac{CDN} infrastructure is also used for live distribution, but end-to-end latency is typically higher than in local direct paths because of ingest, processing, and distribution stages.\cite{peroniEndtoEndPipelinePerspective2025}
\subsubsection{\ac{P2P} video streaming}
\ac{P2P} video streaming distributes media across peers in a decentralized overlay. In this model, a peer can consume stream data and forward it to other peers, reducing direct load on the original producer and potentially improving scalability.

The main disadvantages are greater sensitivity to churn, variable path quality, and possible latency increases caused by multi-hop forwarding.\cite{xuAnalysisCDNP2P2006,meskovicContentDeliveryArchitectures2012} Security and trust are also more complex because peers are not inherently trusted media sources.\cite{SecurityConsiderations}

\subsection{Basic Problems with video streaming}
Core transport problems in video streaming include limited bandwidth, delay jitter, and packet loss rate \cite{apostolopoulosVideoStreamingConcepts}. Overlay forwarding across multiple peers can additionally increase delay variation and out-of-order arrival in practice due to multi-path forwarding and asynchronous peer scheduling.\cite{xuAnalysisCDNP2P2006,meskovicContentDeliveryArchitectures2012}

\subsubsection{Bandwidth}
According to \cite{apostolopoulosVideoStreamingConcepts}:
\begin{quote}
The bandwidth available between two points in the Internet is generally
unknown and time-varying. If the sender transmits faster than the available
bandwidth then congestion occurs, packets are lost, and there is a severe
drop in video quality. If the sender transmits slower than the available
bandwidth then the receiver produces sub-optimal video quality.
\end{quote}

This thesis addresses bandwidth management through two different transport architectures: direct libp2p Streams (\S\ref{sec:stream-transport}) and mesh-based Gossipsub (\S\ref{sec:gossipsub-transport}). Both approaches must handle variable available bandwidth while maintaining acceptable latency and delivery reliability. The evaluation framework tests these architectures across different bitrate and packet-size conditions, described in \S\ref{sec:test-matrix}.
\subsubsection{Delay jitter}
\label{sec:delay-jitter}
According to \cite{apostolopoulosVideoStreamingConcepts}:
\begin{quote}
The end-to-end delay that a packet experiences may fluctuate from packet to
packet. This variation in end-to-end delay is referred to as the delay jitter.
Delay jitter is a problem because the receiver must receive/decode/display
frames at a constant rate, and any late frames resulting from the delay jitter
can produce problems in the reconstructed video, e.g. jerks in the video.
\end{quote}

In practice, this effect is often more pronounced in mesh-like \ac{P2P} overlays. Packets can arrive out of order and must be reordered before decoding. If packets arrive too late for their playback deadline, they are effectively unusable for real-time rendering. Reordering buffers (jitter buffers) mitigate this issue but increase end-to-end latency.\cite{schulzrinne_rtp_2003,xuAnalysisCDNP2P2006}

\subsubsection{Loss rate}
According to \cite{apostolopoulosVideoStreamingConcepts}:
\begin{quote}
A number of different types of
losses may occur, depending on the particular network under consideration.
For example, wired packet networks such as the Internet are afflicted by
packet loss, where an entire packet is erased (lost). On the other hand,
wireless channels are typically afflicted by bit errors or burst errors. Losses
can have a very destructive effect on the reconstructed video quality. To
combat the effect of losses, a video streaming system is designed with error
control.
\end{quote}

In this thesis, loss behavior is measured at the transport level by analyzing gaps in the monotonic packet index carried in each \texttt{VideoStreamChunk} (see \S\ref{sec:chunk-trait}). The metrics and methodology are documented in \S\ref{sec:metric-definitions}.

The implementation applies practical loss-mitigation choices at multiple levels. Low-latency encoder settings are described in \S\ref{sec:gstreamer-recorder}, periodic SPS/PPS insertion (\texttt{h264parse config-interval=1}) supports faster decoder resynchronization after loss, and bounded queues prefer dropping stale buffers over unbounded latency growth. These architectural choices reflect a real-time priority: graceful degradation under loss is preferred over delayed playback of outdated frames.

\section{Handling video streams}
Common stream sources include cameras and computer screens. In raw form, these sources produce data volumes that are impractical for direct network transport.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \begin{quote}A single frame of high definition (1920x1080) video in full color (4 bytes per pixel) is 8,294,400 bytes.\end{quote} \cite{WebVideoCodec2025}
    \item \begin{quote}At a typical 30 frames per second, each second of HD video would occupy 248,832,000 bytes (~249 MB).\end{quote} \cite{WebVideoCodec2025}
    \item \begin{quote}A minute of HD video would need 14.93 GB of storage.\end{quote} \cite{WebVideoCodec2025}
    \item \begin{quote}A fairly typical 30 minute video conference would need about 447.9 GB of storage, and a 2-hour movie would take almost 1.79 TB (that is, 1790 GB).\end{quote} \cite{WebVideoCodec2025}
\end{itemize}

The preceding calculation quantifies raw stream size for sources such as screen capture. The stream therefore must be encoded into a substantially more efficient representation before transport.

A video codec addresses this by exploiting spatial and temporal redundancy, reducing entropy and therefore reducing the total bitrate required for transmission.\cite{apostolopoulosVideoStreamingConcepts,WebVideoCodec2025}

Encoding starts from raw captured frames (for example camera or screen frames), compresses them using codec-specific prediction and transform steps, and produces a compact bitstream while preserving target visual quality. The encoded stream is then packaged into a transport or container format that carries timing and synchronization metadata for playback.\cite{apostolopoulosVideoStreamingConcepts,WebVideoCodec2025}

\subsection{Video codec}
A video codec is software (or hardware logic) that compresses and decompresses digital video. The term "codec" is a portmanteau of "coder and decoder" (or "compressor and decompressor"). Codecs are essential for reducing bitrate and storage requirements while maintaining acceptable visual quality.\cite{WebVideoCodec2025}

Codec selection is a practical trade-off between quality, bitrate efficiency, computational cost, hardware support, and licensing constraints.\cite{WebVideoCodec2025,apostolopoulosVideoStreamingConcepts} Although newer codecs can deliver better compression in some workloads, this thesis focuses on H.264 because it offers broad hardware acceleration support, mature tooling, and predictable interoperability across the used pipeline components.

\subsection{Muxers}
After compression, media streams are multiplexed into a synchronized container or transport stream. The muxer interleaves encoded data and timing metadata so the receiver can reconstruct correct playback order.\cite{apostolopoulosVideoStreamingConcepts,WebVideoCodec2025}

\subsection{Encoding pipeline}
\label{sec:encoding-pipeline}
An encoding pipeline is the ordered sequence of processing stages that transforms uncompressed raw video frames into a compact, network-ready bitstream. Each stage has a well-defined input and output, so individual stages can be replaced by different implementations (software, hardware-accelerated, remote) without affecting the rest of the pipeline. The following description uses H.264 as the concrete codec example; the same conceptual stages apply to all modern block-based codecs (H.265, VP9, AV1), with differences in internal algorithms and compression efficiency.\cite{apostolopoulosVideoStreamingConcepts,WebVideoCodec2025}

\subsubsection{Raw frame acquisition}
The pipeline begins at the capture source. A camera or screen recorder emits an uncompressed raster frame at every tick of the capture clock — typically 24, 30, or 60 times per second. Each frame is a two-dimensional array of pixels stored in a native color format: screen recorders commonly output BGRA (four bytes per pixel), while hardware cameras typically produce NV12 or I420 (YUV planar formats).\cite{WebVideoCodec2025} As shown at the beginning of this section, a single 1080p frame at full color occupies approximately 8 MB, and a continuous 30 fps stream requires around 249 MB per second of raw data. Direct network transmission of such data volumes is impractical; the sole purpose of the subsequent pipeline stages is to reduce this volume as aggressively as possible while preserving perceptual quality.

\subsubsection{Pre-processing}
Before the compressed encoder can accept frames, several normalization steps adapt the raw input to the encoder's expectations:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Frame rate control} --- The capture source may deliver frames at a variable or non-integer rate. The encoder requires a stable, constant frame rate for predictable bitrate and synchronization. A frame rate controller drops surplus frames and duplicates the most recent frame to fill gaps, enforcing the target rate.\cite{apostolopoulosVideoStreamingConcepts}
    \item \textbf{Colorspace conversion} --- H.264 and most video codecs operate in the YCbCr (YUV) color space rather than RGB because human vision is considerably more sensitive to changes in brightness (luma, Y) than to changes in color (chroma, Cb and Cr). Converting to YUV enables \textit{chroma subsampling}: in the 4:2:0 scheme, the two chroma planes are stored at half the horizontal and vertical resolution of the luma plane, halving the chroma data volume with minimal perceptible quality loss. This conversion is a lossless operation for the luma channel and introduces only minor, imperceptible loss in the chroma channels.\cite{WebVideoCodec2025,apostolopoulosVideoStreamingConcepts}
    \item \textbf{Scaling} (optional) --- If the capture resolution differs from the target encoding resolution, the frame is resampled to the desired dimensions using a filter such as bilinear or Lanczos interpolation.\cite{apostolopoulosVideoStreamingConcepts}
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Intra-frame compression}
An \textit{I-frame} (intra-coded frame) is encoded entirely from its own content, without reference to any other frame. It is therefore fully self-contained and can be decoded independently — a property essential for random access, stream join, and error recovery.\cite{apostolopoulosVideoStreamingConcepts,WebVideoCodec2025}

Intra-frame compression exploits spatial redundancy using block prediction, transform coding, and quantization. In practice, this means codecs preserve dominant visual structure while discarding less perceptually important detail to reduce bitrate.\cite{apostolopoulosVideoStreamingConcepts,WebVideoCodec2025} For live streaming, periodic I-frames are critical because they bound recovery time after loss and allow late-joining receivers to synchronize.

\subsubsection{Inter-frame compression}
After the first I-frame, subsequent frames are encoded as \textit{P-frames} (predictive) or \textit{B-frames} (bi-directionally predicted). These exploit \textit{temporal redundancy}: consecutive frames in video of natural scenes are nearly identical because most content moves slowly relative to the frame rate.\cite{apostolopoulosVideoStreamingConcepts,WebVideoCodec2025}

Inter coding estimates motion between frames and stores compact motion vectors plus residual error instead of full frame content. This greatly improves compression, but deeper look-ahead and bidirectional prediction increase buffering delay.\cite{apostolopoulosVideoStreamingConcepts}

The periodic insertion of I-frames is governed by the \textit{Group of Pictures} (GOP) parameter. A shorter GOP generally improves resilience to packet loss and late joins, while a longer GOP improves compression efficiency.\cite{apostolopoulosVideoStreamingConcepts,WebVideoCodec2025}

\subsubsection{Entropy coding}
After transform and quantization, entropy coding removes remaining statistical redundancy losslessly to produce the final compressed bitstream. In H.264, CAVLC prioritizes lower complexity while CABAC provides better compression at higher computational cost.\cite{apostolopoulosVideoStreamingConcepts,WebVideoCodec2025,jesup_rtp_2011}

\subsubsection{Network Abstraction Layer}
The compressed bitstream is organized into \textit{NAL units} (Network Abstraction Layer units, defined in RFC~6184 for RTP transport\cite{jesup_rtp_2011}), which are self-delimiting data units designed to be carried over diverse network environments without knowledge of their contents:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{SPS} (Sequence Parameter Set) --- carries codec-level configuration: profile, level, resolution, frame rate, and decoder buffer requirements. A decoder must receive at least one SPS before it can decode any picture data.\cite{jesup_rtp_2011}
    \item \textbf{PPS} (Picture Parameter Set) --- carries per-picture encoding parameters (e.g., entropy coder selection, deblocking filter settings) referenced by slice headers.\cite{jesup_rtp_2011}
    \item \textbf{Slice NAL units} --- carry the compressed picture data, organized into one or more slices per frame. A slice is a self-contained unit of rows of macroblocks that can be decoded independently of other slices.\cite{jesup_rtp_2011}
\end{itemize}
NAL units are serialized in one of two byte-stream formats: \textit{Annex B} (start-code prefixed, standard for broadcast and file storage) or \textit{AVCC} (length-prefixed, used inside MP4/MOV containers).\cite{jesup_rtp_2011}

\subsubsection{RTP packetization}
For real-time network delivery, NAL units are wrapped into RTP (Real-time Transport Protocol) packets as specified in RFC~6184\cite{jesup_rtp_2011} using the RTP header format defined in RFC~3550\cite{schulzrinne_rtp_2003}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Fragmentation} --- A NAL unit larger than the path MTU (typically around 1400 bytes on Ethernet paths to avoid IP-level fragmentation) is split into \textit{Fragmentation Units} (FU-A), each sized to fit in a single IP datagram.\cite{jesup_rtp_2011}
    \item \textbf{Sequence number} --- A monotonically increasing 16-bit counter in each RTP packet allows the receiver to detect missing packets and restore arrival order, both of which are required before the H.264 decoder can assemble a complete frame.\cite{schulzrinne_rtp_2003}
    \item \textbf{Timestamp} --- A 32-bit timestamp based on a 90~kHz clock encodes the presentation time of the frame carried by the packet, enabling the receiver to schedule frames for display at the correct moment and to detect clock drift.\cite{jesup_rtp_2011,schulzrinne_rtp_2003}
    \item \textbf{SSRC} (Synchronization Source Identifier) --- uniquely identifies the media source, allowing a receiver to demultiplex multiple streams arriving on the same socket.\cite{schulzrinne_rtp_2003}
    \item \textbf{Marker bit} --- set on the last RTP packet of each frame, signaling to the receiver that it may assemble all collected fragments and submit the complete frame to the decoder.\cite{jesup_rtp_2011,schulzrinne_rtp_2003}
\end{itemize}
The resulting RTP datagrams are handed off to the transport layer for delivery to consumers.

\section{Current state of video streaming}
\subsection{Video streaming in live streaming}
Large-scale live streaming platforms such as Twitch or YouTube Live typically use centralized ingest, processing, and \ac{CDN}-based global delivery. This architecture scales reliably and is operationally mature, but it concentrates cost and control at infrastructure providers.\cite{peroniEndtoEndPipelinePerspective2025,huangLiveStreamingIts2023a}

In production, HLS and MPEG-DASH over HTTP remain common due to compatibility with browsers and caching infrastructure. The main trade-off is increased latency compared to direct low-latency transport paths.\cite{pantos_http_2017,noauthor_standards_nodate,noauthor_webrtc_nodate}
\subsubsection{Possibility of censorship}
Centralized delivery also concentrates moderation and governance decisions in a small number of operators, creating dependence on provider policy and availability.\cite{huangLiveStreamingIts2023a,ServiceHealthNov}

Decentralized \ac{P2P} delivery can reduce single-operator dependence, but it still requires authentication, abuse controls, and integrity validation in open overlays.\cite{SecurityConsiderations,WhatAreSecure}
\subsection{Existing \ac{P2P} video streaming solutions}
Existing \ac{P2P} streaming solutions are usually hybrid: peers exchange media when possible, while centralized infrastructure remains for bootstrap, fallback, or coordination. This compromise preserves robustness under variable peer capacity while reducing provider bandwidth demand.\cite{xuAnalysisCDNP2P2006,meskovicContentDeliveryArchitectures2012}

P2P Media Loader illustrates this pattern by adding peer-assisted delivery to browser playback without removing the origin HTTP path.\cite{NovageP2pmedialoader2025} The broader libp2p ecosystem provides reusable decentralized transport primitives, but low-latency live distribution still requires careful handling of timing, reordering, and fan-out behavior.\cite{Libp2pIPFSDocs,SecurityConsiderations}

\subsection{Why fully decentralized live streaming is still uncommon}
Despite the conceptual appeal of \ac{P2P} delivery, fully decentralized live video streaming is still less common in mainstream deployments because live media is unusually sensitive to transport instability.\cite{apostolopoulosVideoStreamingConcepts} Peer churn changes available paths over time, residential uplinks are often asymmetric and limited, and heterogeneous devices contribute highly variable encoding, decoding, and forwarding capacity.\cite{xuAnalysisCDNP2P2006,meskovicContentDeliveryArchitectures2012} In a live scenario, these effects accumulate immediately as delay, jitter, packet loss, or reduced picture quality at the viewer.

There is also a structural conflict between scalability and latency. Mesh-style dissemination improves resilience because content can traverse multiple peers, but every additional forwarding hop can increase queueing delay and reorder packets.\cite{xuAnalysisCDNP2P2006,meskovicContentDeliveryArchitectures2012} Conversely, direct sender-to-receiver paths minimize delay but require the producer to maintain more outgoing connections and upload more duplicate traffic. This trade-off is one of the central architectural tensions in decentralized live streaming, and it directly informs the comparison between Gossipsub and direct libp2p streams in the practical part of this thesis.\cite{apostolopoulosVideoStreamingConcepts}



\section{Implementation of developed video streaming solution}
\subsection{Choice of programming language}
The prototype is implemented in Rust. Besides personal familiarity, Rust was selected for technical reasons that are directly relevant to real-time media systems: memory safety without need garbage collection, strong type system, and predictable performance. High volume packet transport requires high performance, and variable memory allocations and the language gives us guarantee of freeing unused memory as soon as it is no longer needed.

Another important reason is multi-threading safety. The implementation runs concurrent capture, transport, rendering, and analysis tasks, and Rust enforces thread-safety rules at compile time through ownership, borrowing, and the \texttt{Send}/\texttt{Sync} trait system. This significantly reduces risk of data races in shared state. In practice, shared resources are passed through explicit synchronization primitives (for example \texttt{Arc<Mutex<...>>}) or message channels, so synchronization is visible in code and easier to audit.

Rust also provides mature asynchronous tooling (
\texttt{tokio}) and ecosystem crates required by this thesis, namely libp2p for networking and GStreamer bindings for media processing. This makes it possible to keep capture, transport, and rendering in one language while preserving low-level control over buffers, serialization, and timing metadata.

\subsection{Implementation architecture}
The implementation repository is organized as a Rust workspace with reusable library crates and executable binaries. The key design goal is separation of concerns:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Media capture / source layer} --- produces RTP payloads from either live screen capture or a deterministic file source used in testing.
    \item \textbf{Transport layer} --- abstracts 
    \ac{P2P} communication behind a common trait so multiple transport strategies can be evaluated with the same producer and consumer logic.
    \item \textbf{Rendering / sink layer} --- accepts incoming RTP payloads and decodes them to a local video sink.
    \item \textbf{Testing orchestration layer} --- runs producer, renderer, and analyzer roles for repeatable experiments and CSV export.
\end{itemize}

At the transport boundary, each packet is represented by a shared structure called VideoStreamChunk. It contains serialized media bytes, creation timestamp, and monotonic sequence index. This metadata enables the measurement methodology described in Chapter 7 (latency, packet loss, reordering, and throughput). The implementation evaluates two packetization contexts: a live recorder path using \ac{RTP} over MPEG-TS payloading, and a file-based test path using direct H.264-in-\ac{RTP} payloading. This distinction is stated explicitly to separate live-capture integration constraints from controlled transport benchmarking.
\subsection{Screen Recording}
Screen capture is implemented for Wayland using the 
\texttt{ashpd} interface to the XDG desktop portal. This approach delegates monitor/window selection and user permissions to the compositor and portal service.

After the portal session is created, the application obtains a PipeWire file descriptor and selected stream node identifier. These are injected into the GStreamer pipeline through \texttt{pipewiresrc}, so capture frames can be processed with standard media elements.

The recorder pipeline performs rate normalization and encoding before handing payloads to the networking layer:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \texttt{pipewiresrc} $\rightarrow$ \texttt{videorate} $\rightarrow$ \texttt{videoconvert} for acquisition and normalization,
    \item \texttt{timeoverlay} for visible timing/debug annotation,
    \item hardware-first H.264 encoding (VA-API) with automatic fallback to software \texttt{x264enc},
    \item parser/mux/payloader stages that produce RTP payloads consumed via \texttt{appsink}.
\end{itemize}

The implementation uses low-latency settings such as bounded sink buffers and frame dropping when downstream is overloaded, preventing unbounded queue growth during transport congestion.
\subsection{Encoding}
Two dominant open-source media stacks are FFmpeg and GStreamer. For this thesis, GStreamer was chosen because its graph-based pipeline model maps naturally to a modular experimental architecture where individual stages can be replaced without rewriting the full application.

In practice, this enables hardware/software fallback at runtime (for both encoder and decoder), explicit insertion of RTP-related elements, and direct application integration through \texttt{appsink}/\texttt{appsrc}. The same design is used in both recorder and renderer crates, which simplifies debugging and keeps the media path explicit.

The corresponding rendering pipeline accepts RTP H.264 payloads on \texttt{appsrc}, passes them through jitter buffering and depayloading, decodes with hardware-first fallback, converts the video format, and displays frames using \texttt{autovideosink}.
\subsection{Network Layer}
The transport layer is built on libp2p, a modular 
\ac{P2P} stack used in production decentralized systems.
The implementation uses \ac{QUIC} transport and mDNS peer discovery in local test networks, while higher-level dissemination logic is varied per protocol implementation.

All transport variants implement a common trait interface (
\texttt{P2PSwarm}) with two operations: receive inbound chunks and enqueue outbound chunks. This abstraction allows producer/renderer/analyzer components to stay unchanged while only the transport strategy is replaced.

Libp2p was selected because it provides identity, encrypted channels, peer discovery, stream multiplexing, and protocol composition in one ecosystem.\cite{Libp2pIPFSDocs,SecurityConsiderations}
\subsubsection{Gossipsub}
\label{sec:gossipsub-transport}
The first transport variant uses Gossipsub publish/subscribe. Video chunks are serialized and published into a topic-based mesh. Receivers decode messages back into chunk structures and apply freshness filtering before acceptance. In the current codebase, stale chunks are explicitly rejected after a short time-to-live window, which reduces propagation of outdated real-time data.

The expected strength of this variant is resilience: data can propagate even when direct links between producer and all viewers are not available. The expected cost is higher latency and possible reordering caused by multi-hop dissemination.
\subsubsection{Stream}
\label{sec:stream-transport}
The second variant uses direct libp2p streams over a dedicated stream protocol identifier. Outbound chunks are serialized once and sent directly to currently connected peers. Inbound stream payloads are decoded immediately and forwarded to the media pipeline.

This strategy minimizes overlay complexity and serves as a baseline for latency-oriented comparison. Its limitation is scalability in one-to-many scenarios, because the producer must maintain direct outgoing transmission to every consumer.
\subsubsection{Security}
Security in the prototype is based on libp2p secure channels and \ac{QUIC} transport security. In \ac{QUIC}, transport security is provided by \ac{TLS}~1.3, which provides confidentiality, integrity protection, and peer authentication during connection establishment; RFC~9001 also notes that, in the absence of packet loss, new connections can usually be established and secured within a single round trip, which is relevant for real-time media delivery.\cite{thomsonUsingTLSSecure2021} In practice, this means the application does not implement its own key exchange or packet-protection scheme, reducing the risk of cryptographic design and implementation errors. At the same time, secure transport protects the channel between peers, not the higher-level policy of who should be allowed to publish, receive, or forward a stream. \cite{SecurityConsiderations,WhatAreSecure}
\subsubsection{Authentication}
Each node generates a cryptographic peer identity (public/private keypair). This identity is used by libp2p during connection establishment to authenticate the remote peer and, in the Gossipsub variant, to sign published messages so that receivers can verify message origin before accepting or propagating data. This is an authentication mechanism rather than a complete authorization policy: authentication answers which peer established the connection or sent the message, while authorization decides which authenticated peers are allowed to publish, subscribe, or relay a stream. \cite{SecurityConsiderations,WhatAreSecure}

The current prototype does not implement a full authorization policy (e.g., allowlists, role-based access, signed stream ownership claims). It provides authenticated transport primitives and validation hooks that can be extended in future iterations with explicit admission control or peer reputation mechanisms.

\section{Current implementation}
The implementation is organized as a Rust workspace consisting of four library crates and two executable binaries. The library crates handle distinct concerns --- screen capture, rendering, \ac{P2P} network transport, and file-based video reading --- while the binaries integrate them for live use and automated testing respectively.

An early prototype used bare MPEG-TS streams, where each MPEG-TS packet has a fixed size of 188 bytes. Forwarding one network packet per MPEG-TS packet produced an extremely high packet rate that saturated the Gossipsub message queue and caused the stream to stall. The fix was to multiplex several MPEG-TS packets into a single larger RTP datagram: \texttt{mpegtsmux} with \texttt{alignment=7} groups seven 188-byte MPEG-TS packets into one 1316-byte RTP payload, reducing the packet rate by a factor of seven. The file-based testing pipeline goes further and adopts direct H.264-in-RTP payloading (\texttt{rtph264pay}) with a configurable MTU of 4096 bytes, eliminating the MPEG-TS layer entirely.

\subsection{Shared data structures and trait interface}
\label{sec:chunk-trait}
All transport variants carry video data as instances of \texttt{VideoStreamChunk}, defined in \texttt{libraries/network/src/protocol.rs}:

\begin{verbatim}
#[derive(Serialize, Deserialize, Debug, Clone)]
pub struct VideoStreamChunk {
    #[serde(with = "serde_bytes")]
    pub chunk: Vec<u8>,
    pub timestamp: SystemTime,
    pub index: u64,
}
\end{verbatim}

The three fields serve distinct purposes. \texttt{chunk} carries the raw RTP payload bytes; the \texttt{serde\_bytes} attribute instructs the CBOR serializer to encode it as a compact byte array rather than as a sequence of integers, which is important for throughput. \texttt{timestamp} records the wall-clock time at which the chunk was created on the producer; the analyzer subtracts this from its local arrival time to derive per-packet latency. \texttt{index} is a monotonically increasing counter used to detect lost packets (gaps in the sequence) and out-of-order delivery (arrivals where \texttt{index} is less than the highest seen so far).

Serialization between peers uses CBOR via the \texttt{serde\_cbor} crate. CBOR is a compact binary encoding that handles raw byte arrays natively and requires no schema, making it straightforward to extend the structure without a versioning step.

The \texttt{P2PSwarm} trait forms the interface boundary between media-handling and transport code:

\begin{verbatim}
#[async_trait]
pub trait P2PSwarm: Send + Sync {
    fn inbound_rx(&self) -> &Receiver<VideoStreamChunk>;
    async fn send_message(&mut self, chunk: VideoStreamChunk);
}
\end{verbatim}

The trait exposes exactly two operations: read access to an \texttt{async\_channel} receiver that delivers inbound chunks, and an async method to enqueue an outbound chunk. Because all media components interact with the network only through this interface, the transport backend can be selected at runtime without any change to capture, rendering, or analysis code.

\subsection{Screen recording crate}
\label{sec:recorder-crate}
Live screen capture is implemented in \texttt{libraries/recorder} using the \texttt{ashpd} crate, which provides a Rust interface to the XDG Desktop Portal screen-cast service. The portal is the standard Wayland mechanism for delegating monitor and window selection and user permission prompts to the compositor; it is supported by all major Wayland compositors including GNOME Mutter and KDE KWin.

The capture initialization sequence is as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \texttt{Screencast::new()} --- opens a D-Bus proxy to the screen-cast portal.
    \item \texttt{create\_session()} --- creates a portal session object.
    \item \texttt{select\_sources()} --- presents a source picker to the user with cursor embedding and support for both monitors and individual application windows.
    \item \texttt{start()} --- begins capture and returns a stream descriptor containing the PipeWire node ID.
    \item \texttt{open\_pipe\_wire\_remote()} --- returns an \texttt{OwnedFd} (PipeWire file descriptor) for the session.
\end{enumerate}

The file descriptor and node ID are passed directly into the GStreamer pipeline.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1\textwidth]{out/recorder-pipeline/recorder-pipeline.png}
    \caption{Recorder pipeline}
\end{figure}

\label{sec:gstreamer-recorder}
The GStreamer recorder pipeline elements and their relevant parameters are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{\texttt{pipewiresrc}} with \texttt{fd} and \texttt{path} set to the portal-provided file descriptor and PipeWire node ID --- ingests raw frames from PipeWire at the source capture rate.
    \item \textbf{\texttt{queue leaky=downstream}} --- a bounded queue that discards its oldest pending buffers when the downstream pipeline cannot keep up. Without this element, backpressure from a congested transport layer would propagate upstream and stall the compositor's screen capture.
    \item \textbf{\texttt{videorate}}, \textbf{\texttt{videoconvert}} --- normalize the frame rate to a constant target and convert the pixel format to one acceptable to the encoder.
    \item \textbf{\texttt{timeoverlay}} --- renders a wall-clock timestamp into each frame, useful for visual end-to-end latency verification.
    \item \textbf{H.264 encoding, VA-API path:} \texttt{vaapipostproc} converts the frame to a VA-API surface; \texttt{vaapih264enc quality-level=6 bitrate=1000 keyframe-period=0} encodes it via hardware. A \texttt{keyframe-period} of zero instructs the hardware encoder to insert I-frames adaptively rather than at a fixed interval.
    \item \textbf{H.264 encoding, software fallback:} if VA-API elements are unavailable at runtime, the pipeline substitutes an \texttt{identity} pass-through for the post-processor and \texttt{x264enc} (with \texttt{tune=zerolatency}, \texttt{speed-preset=ultrafast}, \texttt{bitrate=500}, and \texttt{keyframe-period=30}) for the encoder. The \texttt{zerolatency} tune disables B-frame look-ahead and internal output buffering, minimizing encoding latency at some cost to compression efficiency.
    \item \textbf{\texttt{h264parse}} with \texttt{config-interval=1} --- instructs the parser to inject SPS and PPS parameter-set NAL units at every key-frame boundary. This allows a newly joining consumer, or one recovering from packet loss, to resynchronize immediately without waiting for the next natural key-frame.
    \item \textbf{\texttt{mpegtsmux alignment=7}} --- multiplexes the H.264 elementary stream into MPEG-TS and groups seven 188-byte MPEG-TS packets per output buffer, yielding 1316-byte payloads.
    \item \textbf{\texttt{rtpmp2tpay pt=33}} --- wraps each MPEG-TS payload in an RTP datagram using payload type 33, the IANA-registered type for MPEG-TS over RTP.
    \item \textbf{\texttt{appsink max-buffers=1 drop=true}} --- exposes RTP samples to application code. A buffer limit of one combined with \texttt{drop=true} discards any sample that arrives while the previous one has not yet been consumed, preventing unbounded memory growth under transport congestion.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Rendering crate}
\label{sec:renderer-crate}
Incoming RTP H.264 payloads are decoded and displayed by the \texttt{GstRenderer} struct in \texttt{libraries/renderer}. The renderer is built around the RTP H.264 format produced by the file-based test source (\texttt{rtph264pay pt=96}) and processes chunks through the following pipeline:

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1\textwidth]{out/renderer-pipeline/renderer-pipeline.png}
    \caption{Renderer pipeline}
\end{figure}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{\texttt{appsrc}} --- configured with RTP H.264 caps (\texttt{encoding-name=H264}, \texttt{payload=96}, and \texttt{clock-rate=90000}) plus \texttt{is-live=true}, \texttt{do-timestamp=false}, and \texttt{format=Time}. Declaring the caps before the first buffer arrives allows GStreamer to negotiate the downstream pipeline topology immediately.
    \item \textbf{\texttt{rtpjitterbuffer latency=5000}} --- accumulates incoming RTP packets for up to 5000 milliseconds and reorders them by sequence number before passing them downstream. The large window absorbs worst-case reordering from Gossipsub's multi-hop mesh, trading initial latency for stable decoding order.
    \item \textbf{\texttt{rtph264depay}}, \textbf{\texttt{h264parse}} --- strip the RTP framing and reassemble NAL units into a continuous H.264 bytestream.
    \item \textbf{H.264 decoding, fallback chain:} \texttt{vaapih264dec low-latency=true} (hardware, VA-API) is tried first; \texttt{v4l2h264dec} (hardware, V4L2) second; \texttt{avdec\_h264} (software, libavcodec) third. The \texttt{low-latency} property on the VA-API decoder disables its internal frame reordering buffer, reducing decoder-introduced latency.
    \item \textbf{\texttt{vaapipostproc}} (optional) --- inserted when available to perform colorspace conversion on the VA-API surface before the software path.
    \item \textbf{\texttt{videoconvert}}, \textbf{\texttt{queue}}, and \textbf{\texttt{autovideosink}} with \texttt{sync=false} --- \texttt{sync=false} instructs GStreamer to display each decoded frame immediately upon arrival, instead of waiting for the presentation clock. For a live \ac{P2P} stream, minimizing end-to-end latency takes priority over smooth frame pacing.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Transport layer crate}
\label{sec:network_crate}
The \texttt{libraries/network} crate provides two concrete implementations of the \texttt{P2PSwarm} trait. Both share the same underlying stack: QUIC transport over UDP listening on all interfaces at a random ephemeral port (\texttt{/ip4/0.0.0.0/udp/0/quic-v1}), and mDNS for automatic peer discovery on local networks. Each node generates a fresh Ed25519 keypair at startup, giving it a unique but ephemeral cryptographic identity for the duration of the session.

\subsubsection{Gossipsub}
The Gossipsub backend configures strict per-message authentication:

\begin{verbatim}
let gossipsub_config = gossipsub::ConfigBuilder::default()
    .validation_mode(gossipsub::ValidationMode::Strict)
    .validate_messages()
    .build()?;
let gs = gossipsub::Behaviour::new(
    MessageAuthenticity::Signed(local_key.clone()),
    gossipsub_config,
)?;
\end{verbatim}

\texttt{ValidationMode::Strict} combined with \texttt{MessageAuthenticity::Signed} requires every Gossipsub message to carry a valid Ed25519 signature from its originating peer before it is forwarded through the mesh. Each inbound message is validated and the outcome is reported back to the swarm: valid, fresh chunks receive \texttt{MessageAcceptance::Accept} and are forwarded; invalid or stale ones receive \texttt{MessageAcceptance::Reject} and are dropped immediately without further propagation.

A 5-second TTL is applied to every accepted chunk. After CBOR deserialization, the implementation computes the chunk's age and rejects it if too old:

\begin{verbatim}
static TTL: Duration = Duration::from_secs(5);
// ...
let age = SystemTime::now()
    .duration_since(chunk.timestamp)
    .unwrap();
if age > TTL {
    // Reject — do not forward through the mesh
}
\end{verbatim}

This prevents stale video frames from continuing to circulate after their playback window has closed, reducing unnecessary load on the mesh.

Peers are managed through mDNS lifecycle events: on \texttt{mdns::Event::Discovered}, each new peer is dialed and added to the Gossipsub mesh via \texttt{add\_explicit\_peer}; on \texttt{mdns::Event::Expired}, it is removed via \texttt{remove\_explicit\_peer}. A shared, thread-safe peer set (\texttt{HashSet<PeerId>} guarded by an \texttt{Arc<RwLock>}) is kept current on every connection event.

\subsubsection{Practical saturation issue in Gossipsub}
\label{sec:gossipsub-saturation}
During development, the main practical issue of the Gossipsub transport was saturation under higher traffic. The observable symptom was that transmission gradually stalled: new chunks were still produced at the sender, but effective real-time delivery degraded until playback stopped progressing.

The bottleneck was not a single failing component but the interaction between packetization and processing cost. In the implementation repository, each inbound Gossipsub message must be deserialized from CBOR into \texttt{VideoStreamChunk} (see \path{libraries/network/src/gossipsub.rs}), and each outbound chunk is serialized and published through the mesh in the same transport backend. When packet rate increased, serialization/deserialization and mesh dispatch competed for runtime time slices and queue capacity.

In practice, this behavior depended strongly on the ratio between bitrate and MTU. Higher bitrate increases the amount of payload generated per second, and smaller MTU splits the same payload into more packets; together they increase message frequency approximately proportionally to:
\[
\text{messages per second} \propto \frac{\text{bitrate}}{\text{MTU}}.
\]
Therefore, the worst saturation occurred at high bitrate with small MTU, while larger MTU at the same bitrate reduced message pressure.

The mitigation was applied in three steps:
\begin{itemize}
    \item inbound CBOR deserialization was moved out of the critical swarm loop into a separate async task, reducing blocking in the main handling path,
    \item MTU was increased (configured through the \texttt{chunk\_size} parameter in \texttt{stream\_test} and through payloader MTU in the media pipelines), reducing packet/message count per second,
    \item bitrate was reduced in unstable scenarios so that offered load stayed below the practical processing limit of the Gossipsub path.
\end{itemize}

This development issue explains why the practical evaluation in \S\ref{sec:test-matrix} varies both bitrate and MTU: these two parameters jointly determine whether Gossipsub remains stable for live media in this prototype. It also explains the observed trend in the analysis chapter (\S\ref{sec:analysis-mtu-1200-comparison}), where larger MTU substantially improves Gossipsub behavior under higher bitrates.

\subsubsection{Stream}
The Stream backend uses \texttt{libp2p\_stream} with a custom versioned protocol identifier:

\begin{verbatim}
const STREAM_PROTOCOL: StreamProtocol
    = StreamProtocol::new("/stream");
\end{verbatim}

On the receiving side, a dedicated Tokio task listens for incoming streams on the \texttt{/stream} protocol, spawning a sub-task per connection. Each sub-task reads the full payload with \texttt{read\_to\_end}, deserializes it from CBOR into a \texttt{VideoStreamChunk}, and forwards the result to the inbound channel.

On the sending side, each \texttt{send\_message} call serializes the chunk into a CBOR byte vector once, then opens a new outbound stream to every peer in the set and writes the same byte vector via \texttt{write\_all}. Serializing once and reusing the byte vector for all destinations avoids redundant encoding work as the consumer count increases.

The same mDNS autodiscovery logic and shared peer set bookkeeping used in the Gossipsub backend are reused unchanged. The Stream backend does not apply a TTL filter; staleness checking is left to the receiving application.

\subsection{Testing binary}
\label{sec:test-binary}
The \texttt{stream\_test} binary is the primary tool for controlled experiments. It accepts two orthogonal command-line arguments via \texttt{clap}:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{\texttt{--protocol}} --- selects the transport backend: \texttt{gossipsub}, \texttt{stream}, or \texttt{hybrid}.
    \item \textbf{\texttt{--role}} --- selects the operational role: \texttt{manager}, \texttt{producer}, \texttt{analyzer}, or \texttt{renderer}.
\end{itemize}

The four roles operate as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Manager} --- spawns two child processes of the same binary: one as \texttt{producer} and one as \texttt{analyzer}, both with the same \texttt{--protocol} flag. It waits for both children to exit or handles Ctrl+C by sending \texttt{SIGINT} to each child before joining them, ensuring clean shutdown of all media pipelines and transport tasks.
    \item \textbf{Producer} --- instantiates a \texttt{VideoReader} (described below) and the chosen transport swarm, then continuously sends every \texttt{VideoStreamChunk} produced by the video pipeline through \texttt{send\_message} until the source reaches end-of-stream.
    \item \textbf{Analyzer} --- subscribes to the inbound channel of the chosen swarm and records each received chunk as an \texttt{AnalysisItem}. After five seconds of inactivity, it writes all collected items to \texttt{Output.csv} using the \texttt{csv} crate:
\begin{verbatim}
#[derive(Serialize)]
pub struct AnalysisItem {
    creation_time: u128,
    arrival_time: u128,
    index: u64,
    size: u32,
}
\end{verbatim}
    \texttt{creation\_time} and \texttt{arrival\_time} are UNIX timestamps in milliseconds; their difference gives per-packet one-way latency. \texttt{index} allows detection of lost and out-of-order packets. \texttt{size} is the chunk payload length plus 24 bytes for the serialized \texttt{timestamp} and \texttt{index} metadata.
    \item \textbf{Renderer} --- receives inbound chunks and pushes each raw byte payload to the renderer backend (\texttt{GstRenderer}). This produces a live decoded video window for visual inspection.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Video reader pipeline}
\label{sec:video-reader-pipeline}
The \texttt{VideoReader} in \texttt{libraries/video-reader} reads a pre-recorded MP4 file specified by the \texttt{VIDEO\_SOURCE} environment variable and emits it as a sequence of RTP H.264 \texttt{VideoStreamChunk} values at the file's original encoded frame rate. Its GStreamer pipeline is designed to produce output in the same RTP H.264 format as a live capture session, keeping test results directly comparable. The pipeline stages are:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \texttt{filesrc} $\rightarrow$ \texttt{qtdemux} --- reads and demultiplexes the MP4 container; the H.264 elementary stream is exposed on a dynamic pad.
    \item \texttt{h264parse} --- normalizes the H.264 bytestream.
    \item \textbf{Decode:} \texttt{vaapih264dec} (hardware, VA-API) with fallback to \texttt{avdec\_h264} (software). Converting to raw frames is required for the subsequent re-encoding step.
    \item \texttt{timeoverlay} --- embeds a wall-clock timestamp for visual end-to-end latency verification.
    \item \textbf{Re-encode:} preferred path: \texttt{vaapih264enc} with quality level 6 and bitrate 500. Fallback path: \texttt{x264enc} configured for zerolatency, ultrafast preset, bitrate 500, and keyframe period 30. Re-encoding produces a fresh H.264 stream with the same encoder settings as the live recorder, ensuring format and bitrate compatibility across test scenarios.
    \item \texttt{h264parse config-interval=1} --- injects inline SPS/PPS parameter sets.
    \item \texttt{rtph264pay mtu=4096 pt=96} --- packetizes NAL units into RTP datagrams with payload type 96 and an MTU of $2^{12}$ = 4096 bytes. The large MTU produces substantially fewer packets per second than the 1316-byte MPEG-TS payloads of the recorder, reducing serialization and dispatch overhead in the transport layer.
    \item \texttt{appsink max-buffers=1 drop=true} --- exposes samples to application code with the same back-pressure policy as the recorder.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Analysis Methodology}
This section describes the systematic approach used to measure and evaluate transport behavior. The methodology comprises five components: the standardized video test source, the factorial test matrix design, the data collection workflow, metric computation from raw packet data, and visualization strategy for comparative interpretation.

\subsection{Test Setup and Data Source}
All experiments use Big Buck Bunny to ensure repeatable and representative measurements. It is a widely recognized open-source test video for streaming research and benchmarking. Big Buck Bunny provides a royalty-free, standardized stream with consistent motion complexity and codec characteristics across repeated runs. Using a file-based source instead of live screen capture eliminates variability caused by changing content, allowing transport behavior to be isolated from capture-side effects.

The video is played through the \texttt{VideoReader} pipeline (described in \S\ref{sec:video-reader-pipeline}), which emits the same RTP H.264 format as the live recorder capture path. This ensures that test results faithfully represent the codec characteristics and packetization behavior of a real live streaming scenario.

\subsection{Test Matrix Design}
\label{sec:test-matrix}
The experimental evaluation employs a factorial test matrix structure to systematically isolate the effects of protocol choice, load, and packet size constraints:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Protocols:} Two transport backends are compared: direct libp2p Streams (point-to-point, \S\ref{sec:stream-transport}) and Gossipsub (mesh-based, \S\ref{sec:gossipsub-transport}).
    \item \textbf{Bitrates:} Four source bitrates are tested: 500 kbps, 2500 kbps, 5000 kbps, and 10000 kbps. These represent a range from low-quality smooth playback to high-quality stalls-prone conditions on constrained links.
    \item \textbf{Packet size (MTU):} Two packet sizes are tested: 1200 bytes (Internet-compatible baseline reflecting typical path MTU constraints) and 4096 bytes (jumbo-frame environments supporting larger payloads end-to-end).
    \item \textbf{Repetitions:} Ten runs per condition ensure statistical confidence in measurements and capture run-to-run variability in transport behavior.
\end{itemize}

This yields a complete matrix of $2 \times 4 \times 2 \times 10 = 80$ experimental runs. The test sequence is orchestrated by a shell script (\texttt{run\_matrix.sh}) that iterates through all combinations and collects results into an organized directory structure.

The selection of bitrates reflects practical deployment scenarios: 500 kbps is typical for mobile or bandwidth-constrained networks; 2500 kbps and 5000 kbps cover standard adaptive bitrate ladder segments; and 10000 kbps represents the upper bound of this transport evaluation, where mesh-based protocols are expected to show increased sensitivity to queueing and scheduling overhead.

The MTU choice is particularly significant. MTU 1200 is chosen as the conservative baseline because it aligns with Internet-standard assumptions and avoids fragmentation across heterogeneous networks. MTU 4096 represents an optimized deployment scenario where jumbo frames are available end-to-end; results at this MTU reveal protocol behavior when packetization overhead is minimized.

\subsection{Data Collection Workflow}
Each experimental run employs the role-based \texttt{stream\_test} binary architecture described in \S\ref{sec:test-binary}. The manager process spawns two child processes, a producer and an analyzer, both with the same \texttt{--protocol} parameter. The producer instantiates the chosen transport swarm and streams Big Buck Bunny through the \texttt{VideoReader} pipeline (\S\ref{sec:video-reader-pipeline}). Each RTP packet is wrapped in a \texttt{VideoStream\-Chunk} with creation timestamp and sequence index. The analyzer subscribes to the inbound channel and logs each received packet as an \texttt{AnalysisItem} record to CSV.

Each \texttt{AnalysisItem} record contains four fields:
\begin{verbatim}
#[derive(Serialize)]
pub struct AnalysisItem {
    creation_time: u128,
    arrival_time: u128,
    index: u64,
    size: u32,
}
\end{verbatim}
The \texttt{creation\_time} and \texttt{arrival\_time} fields (UNIX timestamps in milliseconds) enable computation of one-way packet latency. The \texttt{index} field (packet sequence number) allows detection of lost and out-of-order packets. The \texttt{size} field (chunk payload plus 24 bytes of serialized metadata) reflects the data volume processed by the transport layer.

Both processes run on the same host with shared mDNS peer discovery (no external synchronization service required). One-way latency is computed as the difference between arrival and creation timestamps (assuming host-local clock synchronization). Packet loss is inferred from missing sequence numbers, and reordering is detected by comparing observed sequence order against the monotonically increasing index.

\subsection{Metric Computation Pipeline}
Raw CSV data from each of the 80 runs is processed by a three-stage R-based analysis pipeline:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Per-run aggregation:} For each run's CSV, the pipeline computes run-level summary statistics including mean delay, delay percentiles (p50, p95, p99), jitter (mean and p95 of inter-packet delay deltas), packet loss rate, packet reordering statistics, throughput (goodput), and a composite stability score. These per-run metrics are written to CSV rows.
    
    \item \textbf{Grouping and averaging:} Per-run metrics are then grouped by (protocol, bitrate, MTU) combination. For each group, aggregate statistics are computed: mean and standard deviation of each metric across the ten runs in that group.
    
    \item \textbf{Event extraction:} Supplementary analysis identifies specific events (e.g., delay spikes exceeding a threshold, consecutive out-of-order packets) and exports event-level data for detailed investigation.
\end{enumerate}

The output of this pipeline includes:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \texttt{per\_run\_metrics.csv}: One row per experimental run with all computed metrics.
    \item \texttt{grouped\_metrics.csv}: One row per (protocol, bitrate, MTU) combination with aggregate statistics.
    \item Event CSVs and supporting data for detailed analysis and visualization.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Metric Definitions}
\label{sec:metric-definitions}
The following metrics are computed from raw packet sequences:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{One-way latency (delay):} Defined as $\text{arrival\_time} - \text{creation\_time}$ for each packet, measured in milliseconds. Aggregated statistics include mean, median (p50), 95th percentile (p95), 99th percentile (p99), and coefficient of variation (CV). The p95 and p99 percentiles capture tail latency, which is particularly relevant for real-time streaming because even occasional high-latency packets can disrupt playback.
    
    \item \textbf{Jitter:} Computed as the absolute difference in consecutive packet delays. Mean jitter and p95 jitter are reported. High jitter indicates bursty or irregular delivery patterns, which can stress receiver buffering logic.
    
    \item \textbf{Packet loss:} Inferred from gaps in the sequence number stream. Defined as (missing indices) / (maximum observed index - minimum observed index). Expressed as a percentage.
    
    \item \textbf{Packet reordering rate:} Count of packets received out of sequence as a percentage of total received packets. A packet is out-of-order if its index is less than some previously received index.
    
    \item \textbf{Reorder depth:} For each out-of-order packet, the distance (in positions) the packet moved relative to its correct position. Reported as mean, max, and p95 reorder depth. High reorder depth indicates severe scrambling and requires larger receiver jitter buffers to compensate (see \S\ref{sec:delay-jitter} for discussion of reordering buffers in real-time media).
    
    \item \textbf{Goodput (throughput):} Total bytes successfully received divided by total elapsed time, expressed in Mbps. Reflects the effective data rate delivered to the application and accounts for any overhead or packet loss.
    
    \item \textbf{Stability score:} A composite score on a 0--100 scale computed as $100 - \bigl(0.5 \cdot \min(100, 100 \cdot \text{delay\_CV}) + 0.5 \cdot \min(100, 100 \cdot \text{inter\_arrival\_CV})\bigr)$, then clamped at zero. It combines the coefficient of variation of delay and inter-arrival time with equal weights, so higher scores indicate smoother and more stable delivery while lower scores indicate more variable transport behavior.
    
    \item \textbf{Stutter events and late packet rate:} Identified as gaps in inter-packet arrival time exceeding a threshold (default 120 ms). Each event is logged with duration and frequency, capturing moments when the receiver must wait longer than expected for the next packet. Packets arriving after their playback deadline are treated as late and effectively unusable for real-time rendering; the late packet rate is reported as the ratio of late packets to total received packets.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Visualization Strategy}
Analysis results are presented through a hierarchically organized set of visualizations:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Organization:} Plots are grouped by metric type (delay, packet loss, reordering, throughput, stability) and further split by MTU and bitrate conditions, allowing readers to focus on specific comparisons.
    
    \item \textbf{Color and marker conventions:} Stream protocol results are shown in blue; Gossipsub results in red. This consistent color scheme facilitates quick visual comparison across all plots.
    
    \item \textbf{Scale variants:} Both linear and logarithmic scales are provided for certain metrics (especially delay and jitter) to reveal both absolute magnitudes and proportional differences. Linear scales are effective for near-zero values and absolute measurement; log scales compress wide ranges and highlight relative protocol differences.
    
    \item \textbf{Statistical annotations:} Plots include metadata such as the number of runs per condition ($n = 10$ runs/condition) in titles, supporting transparent reporting of measurement confidence.
    
    \item \textbf{Boxplots for variability:} Key metrics (delay percentiles, reorder depth, stability scores) are shown as boxplots across the ten runs per condition, allowing inspection of run-to-run variability and identification of outliers.
\end{itemize}

This methodology is applied to ten runs per condition using Big Buck Bunny as the consistent video source. Results from the grouped metrics and visualizations are presented in the following Analysis section, organized by MTU profile and deployment perspective to address practical deployment questions.

\section{Analysis}
This section evaluates measured transport behavior using aggregated results from ten runs per condition and the generated plots in the 
\texttt{analysis/plots} directory. The interpretation focuses on three deployment perspectives: (1) MTU 1200 versus MTU 4096, (2) protocol comparison under MTU 1200 as the Internet-compatible baseline, and (3) a shorter MTU 4096 perspective for jumbo-frame capable environments.

The analysis is structured around delay, packet ordering, and stability. Each subsection therefore focuses on one deployment question and then summarizes the operational implication.
\subsection{MTU 1200 versus MTU 4096}
\label{sec:analysis-mtu-1200-comparison}
\subsubsection{Delay behavior}
At a high level, MTU 4096 provides better transport efficiency and lower delay variability, especially for Gossipsub at higher bitrates. However, MTU 1200 remains the practical baseline for Internet transport due to path MTU constraints and fragmentation risk across heterogeneous networks.

For Stream, both MTUs are stable, but MTU 4096 still improves latency. At 10000 kbps, Stream mean one-way delay decreases from 33.08 ms (MTU 1200) to 8.35 ms (MTU 4096), while p95 delay decreases from 123.7 ms to 31.0 ms. Gossipsub shows a much stronger MTU dependency: at 10000 kbps, mean delay drops from 347.95 ms (MTU 1200) to 33.42 ms (MTU 4096), and p95 delay from 1159.8 ms to 94.1 ms.

\subsection{Analysis conclusion}
\label{sec:analysis-conclusion}
The measurements lead to four clear conclusions.

First, MTU 4096 is technically superior for this transport workload, especially for Gossipsub under high bitrate, because it reduces packet processing pressure and significantly improves delay, ordering, and stability metrics.

Second, MTU 1200 must still be treated as the primary deployment target for Internet transport. Therefore, protocol decisions should be guided primarily by MTU 1200 results.

Third, under MTU 1200, Stream provides consistently low latency, negligible reordering, and higher stability scores across all tested bitrates. Gossipsub is viable at lower rates but its stability score falls sharply as bitrate increases, with the 10000 kbps profile showing the weakest behavior.

Fourth, for practical operation with Gossipsub on Internet-compatible MTU 1200 paths, a bitrate cap near 5000 kbps remains a sensible upper bound to avoid the large delay spikes, reordering penalties, and stability degradation observed at 10000 kbps.

\section{Conclusion}
This thesis addressed the design and evaluation of a distributed \ac{P2P} live video streaming solution implemented in Rust. The work focused on replacing centralized distribution assumptions with direct peer-to-peer transport while preserving practical stream quality requirements for interactive playback. The resulting system demonstrates that a modular architecture combining media capture, processing, transport abstraction, rendering, and instrumentation can be implemented in a consistent and reproducible way using contemporary open-source technologies.

The implementation contribution consists primarily in the integration of Wayland-based screen capture, GStreamer media processing, and libp2p networking into a coherent end-to-end pipeline. Particular emphasis was placed on transport modularity, so that different peer-to-peer delivery strategies could be compared under equivalent conditions without changing the surrounding media path. This design made it possible to evaluate protocol behavior at the transport layer while keeping the rest of the application architecture stable, which was essential for drawing meaningful comparative conclusions.

The experimental part of the thesis established a repeatable measurement workflow on Linux systems and collected structured analyzer output for protocol comparison under controlled bitrate and \ac{MTU} conditions. The observed behavior confirms that direct stream-based transport provides more stable low-latency delivery and substantially better packet ordering characteristics across the tested workload profile. In contrast, mesh-based Gossipsub transport remains functional in lower-load scenarios but exhibits reduced suitability for high-throughput real-time media forwarding, particularly when constrained by Internet-compatible packet sizes.

The analysis further indicates that transport performance is strongly coupled with packetization constraints at the network edge. Environments permitting larger effective \ac{MTU} values show improved behavior for both evaluated protocols, while Internet-oriented constraints expose protocol overhead and buffering effects more clearly. From a deployment perspective, this result reinforces the importance of evaluating media transport strategies under conservative network assumptions rather than relying only on favorable local conditions.

From a systems perspective, the thesis demonstrates that decentralized media delivery can be made operational with available Rust and libp2p tooling, but also that protocol choice must be aligned with the communication pattern of real-time video. The tested implementation confirms that not every peer-to-peer overlay mechanism is equally appropriate for continuous media payload transport, even if it is effective for discovery or signaling use cases. This distinction is significant for future decentralized multimedia systems, where architectural separation between control-plane and data-plane responsibilities is likely to be essential.

The thesis objective is therefore considered fulfilled: a functional distributed streaming prototype was implemented, instrumented, and tested, and the evaluation produced actionable conclusions about transport suitability in realistic operating conditions. The presented results contribute practical guidance for selecting peer-to-peer transport mechanisms in Rust-based live video applications and provide a concrete baseline for subsequent research and engineering refinement.

\section{Future work}

This thesis demonstrated that \ac{P2P} video streaming is feasible with contemporary Rust tooling and libp2p frameworks, and identified protocol selection as a critical factor in achieving acceptable latency and delivery consistency. However, the prototype operates under controlled laboratory conditions with a small number of peers and carefully isolated variables. Practical deployment requires further research into operational scale, architectural integration, and enhanced functionality. The following sections outline promising directions that build upon this work to address real-world deployment challenges and extend the system toward production-ready distributed media systems.

\subsection{Hybrid Transport Architectures}
Subsequent research can explore hybrid transport designs that combine Gossipsub and direct stream protocols. Gossipsub can be used primarily as a signaling/discovery layer, while direct streams carry the media payload between selected peers. This architecture may retain mesh resilience while improving end-to-end latency and delivery predictability.
\subsection{Large scale stress testing}
To validate system robustness, future studies should prioritize large-scale stress testing of the \ac{P2P} application. This includes deployment across a high number of geographically distributed peers in real-world network conditions. The objective is to evaluate jitter, latency, and packet loss under high concurrency and to quantify scalability limits under sustained load.
\subsection{Support for sub-streams}
Expanding the protocol to support sub-streams represents a significant functional upgrade. Sub-streams would enable secondary video feeds, such as guest webcams or screen sharing, to operate alongside the primary broadcast. Implementing this feature would transition the protocol from a passive consumption tool into a comprehensive solution for interactive video conferencing and collaborative environments.
