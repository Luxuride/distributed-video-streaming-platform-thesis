\section{Introduction}
This work explores the possibilities of decentralized video streaming using \ac{P2P} technologies. The main goal is to create a functional prototype of a \ac{P2P} video streaming application and evaluate its performance and stability. The motivation behind this work is to address the limitations of traditional centralized video streaming solutions such as expensive infrastructure costs, single points of failure, and scalability issues. By leveraging \ac{P2P} technologies, I aim to test the feasibility of a decentralized approach to video streaming that can potentially offer improved scalability, resilience, and cost-effectiveness. I will also explore downside of \ac{P2P} video streaming such as latency, quality and security concerns.

In current state, most video streaming solutions rely on centralized servers or \ac{CDN}s to distribute video content to users. While these solutions have been effective in delivering high-quality video streams, they also come with several drawbacks. Centralized server providers can suddenly experience outages, leading to service disruptions for users. With the current centralized model, this can lead to most of the Internet being inaccesible during such outages.

It is clear that there is need for more decentralized solutions that are not dependent on single entities. This work aims to contribute to the understanding of how \ac{P2P} technologies can be applied to video streaming and what benefits and challenges they bring.

In this paper I focus on live video streaming using \ac{P2P}.
\section{Background}
\subsection{Peer To Peer}

Peer-to-peer (
\ac{P2P}) networking is communication model where participating nodes (peers) can directly exchange data and services without relying exclusively on a permanently available central server. A widely used formalization defines \ac{P2P} systems through direct resource sharing between autonomous participants and distinguishes them from traditional client--server systems according to role distribution and control centralization.

From architecture perspective, \ac{P2P} systems are typically described as either pure or hybrid. In pure \ac{P2P}, peers provide both control and data-plane functions in decentralized manner. In hybrid \ac{P2P}, selected centralized components are preserved for tasks such as discovery, bootstrap, or coordination, while most payload distribution is delegated to peers. This classification is important in practice because many real systems are designed as hybrids to combine operational control with horizontal scalability.\cite{xuAnalysisCDNP2P2006,meskovicContentDeliveryArchitectures2012}

The main advantages of \ac{P2P} include improved scalability through aggregate upload capacity of many peers, reduced dependency on single infrastructure provider, and potential cost reduction for large-scale content distribution. However, these benefits are accompanied by trade-offs: peer churn, asymmetric residential connectivity, heterogeneous device capabilities, and more complex trust and abuse resistance mechanisms compared to centralized delivery.\cite{xuAnalysisCDNP2P2006,meskovicContentDeliveryArchitectures2012,SecurityConsiderations}

For video distribution, \ac{P2P} is commonly combined with \ac{CDN} infrastructure in hybrid designs. Literature shows this approach can reduce provider bandwidth costs while preserving service stability by using the \ac{CDN} as fallback when peer capacity is insufficient.\cite{xuAnalysisCDNP2P2006,meskovicContentDeliveryArchitectures2012} This hybrid approach is also reflected in modern implementations and frameworks, where transport, peer discovery, and secure communication primitives are exposed by reusable protocol stacks (for example libp2p) and browser-oriented distribution libraries.\cite{Libp2pIPFSDocs,Libp2pEthereumMerge,NovageP2pmedialoader2025}

\subsection{Video Streaming}
Video streaming is method of transmitting video data over a network in a continuous flow, allowing users to watch the video content in real-time without needing to download the entire file beforehand.  

The term covers both "on‑demand" services (pre‑recorded content fetched by the client) and "live" broadcasts where the stream is generated and consumed simultaneously.  In practice the distinction blurs: on‑demand systems still deliver chunks progressively, and live systems may buffer a few seconds of content to allow for network variability.  Historically streaming relied on specialised protocols such as RTSP/RTMP, but in the last decade the industry has converged on HTTP‑based delivery because of ubiquitous firewall/NAT traversal and existing CDN infrastructures.

Streaming differs from a simple progressive download in that the sender continually pushes data while the receiver decodes and presents frames as they arrive; the sender may also adapt the data rate in response to network conditions (see \S\ref{sec:abr}).

\subsubsection{Adaptive Bitrate Streaming}
\label{sec:abr}
Adaptive bitrate (ABR) streaming is the mechanism by which a player dynamically switches between multiple encoded representations of a video depending on the available bandwidth, buffer occupancy and device capabilities. Without ABR, a single high‑quality stream would stall whenever the network cannot sustain the chosen rate, whereas a low‑bitrate stream wastes capacity on good connections. Early ABR algorithms were rate‑based (choose the highest bitrate below the measured throughput), buffer‑based (choose based on playback buffer level) or hybrid combinations; modern systems increasingly employ machine‑learning models trained on large datasets of network traces and QoE measurements. ABR logic is usually implemented client‑side and relies on manifests that list the available bitrates and segment URLs. Poor ABR decisions are one of the primary causes of buffering events, which are key influence factors in QoE models discussed in the previous subsection.

\subsubsection{Streaming Protocols and Formats}
Video streaming is not a single protocol but a family of delivery schemes. The most common today are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{HTTP Live Streaming (HLS)} – developed by Apple and defined in RFC 8216, HLS uses an index playlist (.m3u8) containing URLs of media segments; segments are typically MPEG‑TS or fMP4 files fetched over HTTP/TCP (now QUIC) \cite{pantos_http_2017}. Players periodically reload the playlist to discover new segments.
    \item \textbf{MPEG‑DASH} – an ISO standard that is similar to HLS but uses MPD manifests and supports fMP4 natively; transport is also HTTP/TCP/QUIC \cite{noauthor_standards_nodate}. Its standardization has encouraged decoupling of container format and transport.
    \item \textbf{RTMP/RTSP} – older TCP‑based protocols used for low‑latency live streaming; RTMP carries FLV packets over a persistent TCP connection, while RTSP negotiates RTP/UDP streams. These are still used in encoder‑to‑server links but are being phased out in browsers.
    \item \textbf{WebRTC} – designed for real‑time communication, WebRTC uses SRTP over UDP (or QUIC) and supports congestion control and retransmission schemes; it is the only protocol with broad browser support for sub‑second latency \cite{mcgrew_datagram_2010,noauthor_webrtc_nodate}.
\end{itemize}
Each protocol imposes different requirements on the underlying transport layer: HTTP‑based approaches inherit TCP’s in‑order delivery and congestion control, whereas RTP/UDP‑based systems expose packet loss and jitter directly to the application. Understanding these characteristics is crucial when designing a P2P transport since the replacement must either emulate or improve upon them.


\subsubsection{Video Streaming Pipeline}
The following quote explains the end-to-end video streaming pipeline, which covers the entire process from video capture to playback on the user's device.
\begin{quote}
The end-to-end streaming pipeline starts at the ingestion stage with the capture of raw video by a camera-equipped device, with a codec applying spatial and temporal compression to the raw footage for reducing the video size, and subsequent upload of the encoded video over the Internet to a media server.

(\dots)

The processing stage, which primarily involves internal operations within the media server, handles the storage and transformation of ingested video. Transformation tasks, such as video segmentation and transcoding, enable the pipeline to manage heterogeneity in network connectivity and device capabilities. Video segmentation divides the video into smaller chunks, while transcoding converts these chunks into multiple representations with different resolutions, bitrates, and frame rates.

(\dots)

The distribution stage handles video delivery from the media server to a user device, which decodes and plays back the content. In addition to ISPs providing network connectivity, this stage involves CDNs that disseminate video from their edge servers to user devices with low latency and high QoE. To address the heterogeneity of user devices and variable network conditions, a media player on the user device runs an ABR algorithm. Among the components of the end-to-end pipeline, ABR, QoE, and CDN aspects attract the most research efforts. QoE models express users’ subjective experience as a function of measurable influence factors (IFs) like stall duration and video quality. They rely on subjective testing and user experience design. Network engineering ensures low latency and high bitrate, which QoE models account for directly or through other IFs. Data science enhances their predictive power with learning-based techniques.
\end{quote}\cite{peroniEndtoEndPipelinePerspective2025}

My work mainly focuses on the distribution stage of the video streaming pipeline, specifically on the transport layer that delivers video content from the media server to user devices. I explore how \ac{P2P} technologies can be utilized in this stage to create a decentralized video streaming solution. However, I also touch upon other aspects of the video streaming pipeline, such as video encoding and decoding, as in my implementation they influence viability of my transport layer solution.

Modern distribution pipelines also generate manifests (playlists) and implement adaptive‑bitrate (ABR) logic in the player, which selects among multiple encoded representations based on observed throughput and buffer state (see \S\ref{sec:abr}). Manifest protocols such as HLS and MPEG‑DASH are transported over HTTP, riding on top of TCP or QUIC, which means that any P2P replacement needs to interoperate with or replicate these behaviours.

The basic idea of video streaming is to split the video
into parts, transmit these parts in succession, and enable the receiver to
decode and playback the video as these parts are received, without having to
wait for the entire video to be delivered.
\end{quote}~\cite{apostolopoulosVideoStreamingConcepts}

It is a method of video data transmission from server to client. From data transport perspective, video streaming can be broken down into 3 main parts:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Producer - Creates video stream to be provided to consumers
    \item Transport layer - Provides provided video stream to all consumers
    \item Consumer - Accepts video stream from transport layer
\end{itemize}
We can later use these these parts when discussing various transport layer implementations.

\subsubsection{Static video streaming}
Static video streaming is method of video streaming where the video content is pre-recorded and stored on a server. The server then delivers the video content to clients upon request.

This method allows for efficient use of bandwidth and decrease of latency, as the video can be stored on \ac{CDN} located closer to the user.


\subsubsection{Live video streaming}
Live video streaming is method of video streaming where the video content is captured and transmitted in real-time. This means there is no video file but rather a continuous stream of video data being sent from the producer to the consumer.

According to Cloudflare\cite{WhatVideoCDN} \ac{CDN} can also be used to distribute the live stream to multiple viewers, but the latency is usually higher compared to static video streaming due to the real-time nature of the content.
\subsubsection{\ac{P2P} video streaming}
\ac{P2P} video streaming is method of video streaming where the video content is distributed among multiple peers in a decentralized manner. In this model, each peer can act as both a producer and a consumer of video content in the transport layer. This means that when a peer receives a video stream, it can also share that stream with other peers in the network, reducing the load on the original producer and improving scalability.

The clear downsides of \ac{P2P} video streaming are increased latency and potential quality issues due to the decentralized nature of the network. Additionally, security concerns may arise as peers may not be trustworthy sources of video content.

\subsection{Basic Problems with video streaming}
The basic problems of video streaming are bandwidth, delay jitter and loss rate. \cite{apostolopoulosVideoStreamingConcepts} There are also other problems in mesh networks like some implementations of \ac{P2P} streaming, which can also introduce packet order jitter.

\subsubsection{Bandwidth}
\begin{quote}
The bandwidth available between two points in the Internet is generally
unknown and time-varying. If the sender transmits faster than the available
bandwidth then congestion occurs, packets are lost, and there is a severe
drop in video quality. If the sender transmits slower than the available
bandwidth then the receiver produces sub-optimal video quality.
\end{quote}~\cite{apostolopoulosVideoStreamingConcepts}
\subsubsection{Delay jitter}
\begin{quote}
The end-to-end delay that a packet experiences may fluctuate from packet to
packet. This variation in end-to-end delay is referred to as the delay jitter.
Delay jitter is a problem because the receiver must receive/decode/display
frames at a constant rate, and any late frames resulting from the delay jitter
can produce problems in the reconstructed video, e.g. jerks in the video.
\end{quote}~\cite{apostolopoulosVideoStreamingConcepts}

It is mostly pronounced in mesh networks like some \ac{P2P} implementations. Packets may arrive out of order due to the decentralized nature of the network. This can lead to packet loss due to packet being unpresentable at the time of arrival, as the receiver must reorder packets before decoding and rendering the video stream. Packet jitter can be mitigated through techniques such as jitterbuffer, but it introduces latency at the cost of stable playback.

\subsubsection{Loss rate}
\begin{quote}
A number of different types of
losses may occur, depending on the particular network under consideration.
For example, wired packet networks such as the Internet are afflicted by
packet loss, where an entire packet is erased (lost). On the other hand,
wireless channels are typically afflicted by bit errors or burst errors. Losses
can have a very destructive effect on the reconstructed video quality. To
combat the effect of losses, a video streaming system is designed with error
control.
\end{quote}~\cite{apostolopoulosVideoStreamingConcepts}

\section{Handling video streams}
Common stream sources for streaming are camera or computer screen. In raw form, these data sources are not viable for network transport due to their large sizes.

\begin{itemize}
    \item "A single frame of high definition (1920x1080) video in full color (4 bytes per pixel) is 8,294,400 bytes." \cite{WebVideoCodec2025}
    \item "At a typical 30 frames per second, each second of HD video would occupy 248,832,000 bytes (~249 MB)." \cite{WebVideoCodec2025}
    \item "A minute of HD video would need 14.93 GB of storage." \cite{WebVideoCodec2025}
    \item "A fairly typical 30 minute video conference would need about 447.9 GB of storage, and a 2-hour movie would take almost 1.79 TB (that is, 1790 GB)." \cite{WebVideoCodec2025}
\end{itemize}

The prepending calculation describes the size of raw video stream that we can get from source like screen recording. Because of that we need to first encode these streams into more effitient format.

For that we use a video codec, which handles the problem of large video files, by encoding video into more predictive patterns, decreasing overall size of the stream.
It is decreasing entropy of the video stream and removing redundant information.

The process of encoding video starts with the raw video data captured from the source (e.g., camera or screen recording). We use codec to compress this raw video data into a more compact format. The codec analyzes the video frames and identifies patterns, redundancies, and similarities between frames. It then applies various compression techniques to reduce the amount of data needed to represent the video while trying to maintain level of quality based on the encoding settings. The last step of encoding is packaging the compressed video data into a container format, which includes metadata and timing information for synchronization during playback.

\subsection{Video codec}
Video codec is software that compresses and decompresses digital video. The term "codec" is a portmanteau of "coder-decoder" or "compressor-decompressor." Video codecs are essential for reducing the size of video files, making them easier to store and transmit over networks.

All video codecs work by optimizing the representation of video data and therefore reducing entropy of the video stream.
\begin{quote}
As is the case with any encoder, there are two basic groups of factors affecting the size and quality of the encoded video: specifics about the source video's format and contents, and the characteristics and configuration of the codec used while encoding the video.
\end{quote}~\cite{WebVideoCodec2025}

\subsection{Muxers}
After the video and audio have been separately compressed by their respective codecs, they need to be combined into a single, synchronized package for delivery. This process is called multiplexing, or muxing.

A muxer interleaves the compressed video and audio streams (along with any other data, like subtitles or chapter markers) into a single file or stream, known as a container format. The container file includes timing information and metadata that tells the consumer's video player how to synchronize the different streams so that the audio perfectly matches the video playback.

\subsection{Encoding pipeline}
\label{sec:encoding-pipeline}
An encoding pipeline is the ordered sequence of processing stages that transforms uncompressed raw video frames into a compact, network-ready bitstream. Each stage has a well-defined input and output, so individual stages can be replaced by different implementations (software, hardware-accelerated, remote) without affecting the rest of the pipeline. The following description uses H.264 as the concrete codec example; the same conceptual stages apply to all modern block-based codecs (H.265, VP9, AV1), with differences in internal algorithms and compression efficiency.\cite{apostolopoulosVideoStreamingConcepts,WebVideoCodec2025}

\subsubsection{Raw frame acquisition}
The pipeline begins at the capture source. A camera or screen recorder emits an uncompressed raster frame at every tick of the capture clock — typically 24, 30, or 60 times per second. Each frame is a two-dimensional array of pixels stored in a native color format: screen recorders commonly output BGRA (four bytes per pixel), while hardware cameras typically produce NV12 or I420 (YUV planar formats).\cite{WebVideoCodec2025} As shown at the beginning of this section, a single 1080p frame at full color occupies approximately 8 MB, and a continuous 30 fps stream requires around 249 MB per second of raw data. Direct network transmission of such data volumes is impractical; the sole purpose of the subsequent pipeline stages is to reduce this volume as aggressively as possible while preserving perceptual quality.

\subsubsection{Pre-processing}
Before the compressed encoder can accept frames, several normalization steps adapt the raw input to the encoder's expectations:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Frame rate control} --- The capture source may deliver frames at a variable or non-integer rate. The encoder requires a stable, constant frame rate for predictable bitrate and synchronization. A frame rate controller drops surplus frames and duplicates the most recent frame to fill gaps, enforcing the target rate.\cite{apostolopoulosVideoStreamingConcepts}
    \item \textbf{Colorspace conversion} --- H.264 and most video codecs operate in the YCbCr (YUV) color space rather than RGB because human vision is considerably more sensitive to changes in brightness (luma, Y) than to changes in color (chroma, Cb and Cr). Converting to YUV enables \textit{chroma subsampling}: in the 4:2:0 scheme, the two chroma planes are stored at half the horizontal and vertical resolution of the luma plane, halving the chroma data volume with minimal perceptible quality loss. This conversion is a lossless operation for the luma channel and introduces only minor, imperceptible loss in the chroma channels.\cite{WebVideoCodec2025,apostolopoulosVideoStreamingConcepts}
    \item \textbf{Scaling} (optional) --- If the capture resolution differs from the target encoding resolution, the frame is resampled to the desired dimensions using a filter such as bilinear or Lanczos interpolation.\cite{apostolopoulosVideoStreamingConcepts}
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Intra-frame compression}
An \textit{I-frame} (intra-coded frame) is encoded entirely from its own content, without reference to any other frame. It is therefore fully self-contained and can be decoded independently — a property essential for random access, stream join, and error recovery.\cite{apostolopoulosVideoStreamingConcepts,WebVideoCodec2025}

Intra-frame compression exploits \textit{spatial redundancy}: adjacent pixels in a natural image are highly correlated, and a block of pixels can be predicted from its neighbors with small residual error. The process proceeds as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item The frame is partitioned into macroblocks of 16$\times$16 pixels (H.264) or larger variable-size coding tree units (H.265, AV1).\cite{jesup_rtp_2011,WebVideoCodec2025}
    \item \textit{Intra prediction} estimates each block by extrapolating from already-encoded neighboring blocks within the same frame, using one of several directional prediction modes. Only the difference (residual) between the prediction and the actual block is passed to the next step.\cite{apostolopoulosVideoStreamingConcepts}
    \item A \textit{Discrete Cosine Transform} (DCT) converts each residual block from the spatial domain to the frequency domain, concentrating most of the signal energy into a small number of low-frequency coefficients while high-frequency coefficients remain near zero.\cite{apostolopoulosVideoStreamingConcepts,WebVideoCodec2025}
    \item \textit{Quantization} rounds each frequency coefficient to a configurable step size. High-frequency components — which represent fine texture detail that is largely invisible at normal viewing distances — are discarded with the most aggressive rounding. This is the primary lossy step in the pipeline; the quantization parameter (QP) or constant-rate factor (CRF) directly controls the trade-off between output bitrate and visual quality.\cite{WebVideoCodec2025}
\end{enumerate}
The quantized coefficients together with the chosen prediction modes constitute the compressed I-frame data.

\subsubsection{Inter-frame compression}
After the first I-frame, subsequent frames are encoded as \textit{P-frames} (predictive) or \textit{B-frames} (bi-directionally predicted). These exploit \textit{temporal redundancy}: consecutive frames in video of natural scenes are nearly identical because most content moves slowly relative to the frame rate.\cite{apostolopoulosVideoStreamingConcepts,WebVideoCodec2025}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Motion estimation} --- For each macroblock in the current frame the encoder searches a reference frame (an already-encoded earlier frame, or for B-frames both an earlier and a later frame) for the best-matching block within a search window. The displacement between the current and reference block positions is stored as a compact \textit{motion vector}.\cite{apostolopoulosVideoStreamingConcepts}
    \item \textbf{Motion compensation} --- The encoder fetches the predicted block from the reference frame using the motion vector and subtracts it from the actual block, yielding a small \textit{residual}. Only this residual — not the full block content — is passed through DCT and quantization, achieving dramatically higher compression than intra coding alone.\cite{apostolopoulosVideoStreamingConcepts}
    \item \textbf{B-frames} require the encoder to buffer future frames before the current frame can be encoded, introducing additional latency proportional to the look-ahead window. For this reason B-frames are often disabled in real-time streaming encoders where minimizing end-to-end latency takes priority over maximum compression efficiency.\cite{apostolopoulosVideoStreamingConcepts}
\end{itemize}

The periodic insertion of I-frames is governed by the \textit{Group of Pictures} (GOP) parameter. A shorter GOP improves resilience to packet loss and reduces the penalty for late stream joins, while a longer GOP allows more inter-frame references and better overall compression. Live streaming systems typically use GOP lengths of one to four seconds.\cite{apostolopoulosVideoStreamingConcepts,WebVideoCodec2025}

\subsubsection{Entropy coding}
After quantization, the resulting integers (DCT coefficients, motion vectors, and codec syntax elements) still exhibit statistical structure: many coefficients are zero, and non-zero values cluster near the DC component. Entropy coding removes this redundancy losslessly, mapping frequently occurring patterns to shorter bit sequences.\cite{apostolopoulosVideoStreamingConcepts} H.264 supports two entropy coders:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{CAVLC} (Context-Adaptive Variable-Length Coding) --- lower computational complexity, used in the H.264 Baseline and Constrained Baseline profiles.\cite{jesup_rtp_2011}
    \item \textbf{CABAC} (Context-Adaptive Binary Arithmetic Coding) --- approximately 10--15\% better compression than CAVLC for the same visual quality, used in the Main and High profiles at the cost of higher encoder and decoder complexity.\cite{WebVideoCodec2025,jesup_rtp_2011}
\end{itemize}
The output of entropy coding is the final compressed H.264 bitstream.

\subsubsection{Network Abstraction Layer}
The compressed bitstream is organized into \textit{NAL units} (Network Abstraction Layer units, defined in RFC~6184 for RTP transport\cite{jesup_rtp_2011}), which are self-delimiting data units designed to be carried over diverse network environments without knowledge of their contents:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{SPS} (Sequence Parameter Set) --- carries codec-level configuration: profile, level, resolution, frame rate, and decoder buffer requirements. A decoder must receive at least one SPS before it can decode any picture data.\cite{jesup_rtp_2011}
    \item \textbf{PPS} (Picture Parameter Set) --- carries per-picture encoding parameters (e.g., entropy coder selection, deblocking filter settings) referenced by slice headers.\cite{jesup_rtp_2011}
    \item \textbf{Slice NAL units} --- carry the compressed picture data, organized into one or more slices per frame. A slice is a self-contained unit of rows of macroblocks that can be decoded independently of other slices.\cite{jesup_rtp_2011}
\end{itemize}
NAL units are serialized in one of two byte-stream formats: \textit{Annex B} (start-code prefixed, standard for broadcast and file storage) or \textit{AVCC} (length-prefixed, used inside MP4/MOV containers).\cite{jesup_rtp_2011}

\subsubsection{RTP packetization}
For real-time network delivery, NAL units are wrapped into RTP (Real-time Transport Protocol) packets as specified in RFC~6184\cite{jesup_rtp_2011} using the RTP header format defined in RFC~3550\cite{schulzrinne_rtp_2003}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Fragmentation} --- A NAL unit larger than the path MTU (typically around 1400 bytes on Ethernet paths to avoid IP-level fragmentation) is split into \textit{Fragmentation Units} (FU-A), each sized to fit in a single IP datagram.\cite{jesup_rtp_2011}
    \item \textbf{Sequence number} --- A monotonically increasing 16-bit counter in each RTP packet allows the receiver to detect missing packets and restore arrival order, both of which are required before the H.264 decoder can assemble a complete frame.\cite{schulzrinne_rtp_2003}
    \item \textbf{Timestamp} --- A 32-bit timestamp based on a 90~kHz clock encodes the presentation time of the frame carried by the packet, enabling the receiver to schedule frames for display at the correct moment and to detect clock drift.\cite{jesup_rtp_2011,schulzrinne_rtp_2003}
    \item \textbf{SSRC} (Synchronization Source Identifier) --- uniquely identifies the media source, allowing a receiver to demultiplex multiple streams arriving on the same socket.\cite{schulzrinne_rtp_2003}
    \item \textbf{Marker bit} --- set on the last RTP packet of each frame, signaling to the receiver that it may assemble all collected fragments and submit the complete frame to the decoder.\cite{jesup_rtp_2011,schulzrinne_rtp_2003}
\end{itemize}
The resulting RTP datagrams are handed off to the transport layer for delivery to consumers.

\section{Current state of video streaming}
\subsection{Video streaming in video calls}
Video calls are a common use case for real-time video streaming, but they represent a different optimization problem than one-to-many live broadcasting. In a video call, the primary objective is very low end-to-end latency and stable bidirectional interaction between a relatively small number of participants. For this reason, modern systems commonly rely on centralized infrastructure that coordinates session establishment, assists with network address translation traversal, and often relays or selectively forwards media streams between participants. Browser-based real-time communication is today strongly associated with WebRTC, which standardizes media exchange and security mechanisms for interactive communication on the web.\cite{noauthor_webrtc_nodate,mcgrew_datagram_2010}

Although direct peer-to-peer communication can be used for very small groups, the fully connected topology scales poorly because each participant must upload a separate stream to multiple receivers. As the number of participants grows, both aggregate upload requirements and coordination complexity increase rapidly. Centralized mediation therefore remains the dominant architectural choice for video conferencing because it provides predictable quality, simpler access control, recording, moderation, and easier operation under heterogeneous client and network conditions.\cite{peroniEndtoEndPipelinePerspective2025,huangLiveStreamingIts2023a}
\subsection{Video streaming in live streaming}
Large-scale live streaming platforms such as Twitch or YouTube Live solve a different problem: one producer providing content to many consumers. In this scenario, the dominant architecture is still centralized ingest followed by processing and global distribution through \ac{CDN} infrastructure. The source stream is received by an origin service, frequently transcoded into multiple resolutions, segmented into chunks, and then replicated to edge servers close to viewers. This model is operationally expensive, but it offers strong control over quality, observability, and overall stability.\cite{WhatLiveStreaming,WhatVideoCDN,peroniEndtoEndPipelinePerspective2025}

The protocols most commonly used in production platforms, especially HLS and MPEG-DASH, are built around HTTP delivery of media segments. This makes them highly compatible with browsers, caches, enterprise networks, and existing \ac{CDN} deployments. The trade-off is latency: segment-based delivery is robust and scalable, but usually operates with several seconds of delay unless additional low-latency extensions are employed. When lower latency is required, platforms often introduce specialized transports such as WebRTC, but doing so usually increases infrastructure cost and operational complexity.\cite{pantos_http_2017,noauthor_standards_nodate,noauthor_webrtc_nodate}
\subsubsection{Possibility of censorship}
An important consequence of centralized live streaming is that technical delivery, moderation, and platform governance are concentrated in a small number of operators. Content creators therefore depend not only on the availability of the provider's infrastructure, but also on the provider's business rules, moderation policies, and regional restrictions. Temporary outages of major cloud or platform providers also demonstrate that centralized dependencies can affect availability on a wide scale.\cite{huangLiveStreamingIts2023a,ServiceHealthNov}

Decentralized \ac{P2P} video streaming can partially mitigate this concentration of control by removing the requirement for a single distributor of media. However, decentralization does not eliminate governance problems; it shifts them. A fully decentralized system still needs mechanisms for peer authentication, abuse mitigation, access control, and stream authenticity verification. These concerns are particularly important in open overlays where unknown peers may inject invalid or malicious traffic.\cite{SecurityConsiderations,WhatAreSecure}
\subsection{Existing \ac{P2P} video streaming solutions}
Existing \ac{P2P} solutions rarely attempt to replace the entire streaming stack. Instead, they usually adopt a hybrid approach in which peers exchange media data opportunistically while centralized servers remain available for bootstrap, fallback delivery, coordination, or metadata distribution. This is also consistent with the literature: hybrid \ac{CDN}-\ac{P2P} architectures are repeatedly described as a practical compromise because they preserve service continuity when peer capacity is insufficient while still reducing bandwidth demand on the provider side.\cite{xuAnalysisCDNP2P2006,meskovicContentDeliveryArchitectures2012}

P2P Media Loader is a representative example of this design. It integrates peer-assisted distribution into browser-based players, but it does not remove the role of the origin server or HTTP delivery path. Instead, peers exchange chunks when possible and the player falls back to conventional delivery when peer data is unavailable or late. This design choice is significant: it shows that current production-oriented \ac{P2P} streaming systems prioritize compatibility and robustness over strict decentralization.\cite{NovageP2pmedialoader2025}

The broader libp2p ecosystem demonstrates that decentralized transport primitives, peer identity, encryption, and stream multiplexing are already available as reusable building blocks. What is still largely missing is a mature, general-purpose architecture for low-latency live video distribution that combines these primitives with media-specific requirements such as timing guarantees, packet reordering tolerance, and efficient fan-out. This gap motivates the implementation work in this thesis.\cite{Libp2pIPFSDocs,Libp2pEthereumMerge,SecurityConsiderations}

\subsection{Why fully decentralized live streaming is still uncommon}
Despite the conceptual appeal of \ac{P2P} delivery, fully decentralized live video streaming remains uncommon in mainstream deployments because live media is unusually sensitive to transport instability. Peer churn changes available paths over time, residential uplinks are often asymmetric and limited, and heterogeneous devices contribute highly variable encoding, decoding, and forwarding capacity. In a live scenario, these effects accumulate immediately as delay, jitter, packet loss, or reduced picture quality at the viewer.\cite{apostolopoulosVideoStreamingConcepts,xuAnalysisCDNP2P2006,meskovicContentDeliveryArchitectures2012}

There is also a structural conflict between scalability and latency. Mesh-style dissemination improves resilience because content can traverse multiple peers, but every additional forwarding hop can increase queueing delay and reorder packets. Conversely, direct sender-to-receiver paths minimize delay but require the producer to maintain more outgoing connections and upload more duplicate traffic. This trade-off is one of the central architectural tensions in decentralized live streaming, and it directly informs the comparison between Gossipsub and direct libp2p streams in the practical part of this thesis.



\section{Implementation of developed video streaming solution}
\subsection{Choice of programming language}
The prototype is implemented in Rust. Besides personal familiarity, Rust was selected for technical reasons that are directly relevant to real-time media systems: memory safety without need garbage collection, strong type system, and predictable performance. High volume packet transport requires high performance, and variable memory allocations and the language gives us guarantee of freeing unused memory as soon as it is no longer needed.

Another important reason is multi-threading safety. The implementation runs concurrent capture, transport, rendering, and analysis tasks, and Rust enforces thread-safety rules at compile time through ownership, borrowing, and the \texttt{Send}/\texttt{Sync} trait system. This significantly reduces risk of data races in shared state. In practice, shared resources are passed through explicit synchronization primitives (for example \texttt{Arc<Mutex<...>>}) or message channels, so synchronization is visible in code and easier to audit.

Rust also provides mature asynchronous tooling (
\texttt{tokio}) and ecosystem crates required by this thesis, namely libp2p for networking and GStreamer bindings for media processing. This makes it possible to keep capture, transport, and rendering in one language while preserving low-level control over buffers, serialization, and timing metadata.

\subsection{Implementation architecture}
The implementation repository is organized as a Rust workspace with reusable library crates and executable binaries. The key design goal is separation of concerns:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Media capture / source layer} --- produces RTP payloads from either live screen capture or a deterministic file source used in testing.
    \item \textbf{Transport layer} --- abstracts 
    \ac{P2P} communication behind a common trait so multiple transport strategies can be evaluated with the same producer and consumer logic.
    \item \textbf{Rendering / sink layer} --- accepts incoming RTP payloads and decodes them to a local video sink.
    \item \textbf{Testing orchestration layer} --- runs producer, renderer, and analyzer roles for repeatable experiments and CSV export.
\end{itemize}

At the transport boundary, packets are represented by a shared structure (\texttt{VideoStreamChunk}) containing serialized media bytes, creation timestamp, and monotonic sequence index. This metadata enables the measurement methodology described in Chapter 7 (latency, packet loss, reordering, and throughput).
\subsection{Screen Recording}
Screen capture is implemented for Wayland using the 
\texttt{ashpd} interface to the XDG desktop portal. This approach delegates monitor/window selection and user permissions to the compositor and portal service.

After the portal session is created, the application obtains a PipeWire file descriptor and selected stream node identifier. These are injected into the GStreamer pipeline through \texttt{pipewiresrc}, so capture frames can be processed with standard media elements.

The recorder pipeline performs rate normalization and encoding before handing payloads to the networking layer:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \texttt{pipewiresrc} $\rightarrow$ \texttt{videorate} $\rightarrow$ \texttt{videoconvert} for acquisition and normalization,
    \item \texttt{timeoverlay} for visible timing/debug annotation,
    \item hardware-first H.264 encoding (VA-API) with automatic fallback to software \texttt{x264enc},
    \item parser/mux/payloader stages that produce RTP payloads consumed via \texttt{appsink}.
\end{itemize}

The implementation uses low-latency settings such as bounded sink buffers and frame dropping when downstream is overloaded, preventing unbounded queue growth during transport congestion.
\subsection{Encoding}
Two dominant open-source media stacks are FFmpeg and GStreamer. For this thesis, GStreamer was chosen because its graph-based pipeline model maps naturally to a modular experimental architecture where individual stages can be replaced without rewriting the full application.

In practice, this enables hardware/software fallback at runtime (for both encoder and decoder), explicit insertion of RTP-related elements, and direct application integration through \texttt{appsink}/\texttt{appsrc}. The same design is used in both recorder and renderer crates, which simplifies debugging and keeps the media path explicit.

The corresponding rendering pipeline accepts RTP H.264 payloads on \texttt{appsrc}, passes them through jitter buffering and depayloading, decodes with hardware-first fallback, converts the video format, and displays frames using \texttt{autovideosink}.
\subsection{Network Layer}
The transport layer is built on libp2p, a modular 
\ac{P2P} stack used in production decentralized systems.
The implementation uses QUIC transport and mDNS peer discovery in local test networks, while higher-level dissemination logic is varied per protocol implementation.

All transport variants implement a common trait interface (
\texttt{P2PSwarm}) with two operations: receive inbound chunks and enqueue outbound chunks. This abstraction allows producer/renderer/analyzer components to stay unchanged while only the transport strategy is replaced.

Libp2p was selected because it provides identity, encrypted channels, peer discovery, stream multiplexing, and protocol composition in one ecosystem 
\cite{Libp2pIPFSDocs,Libp2pEthereumMerge}.
\subsubsection{Gossipsub}
The first transport variant uses Gossipsub publish/subscribe. Video chunks are serialized and published into a topic-based mesh. Receivers decode messages back into chunk structures and apply freshness filtering before acceptance. In the current codebase, stale chunks are explicitly rejected after a short time-to-live window, which reduces propagation of outdated real-time data.

The expected strength of this variant is resilience: data can propagate even when direct links between producer and all viewers are not available. The expected cost is higher latency and possible reordering caused by multi-hop dissemination.
\subsubsection{Stream}
The second variant uses direct libp2p streams over a dedicated stream protocol identifier. Outbound chunks are serialized once and sent directly to currently connected peers. Inbound stream payloads are decoded immediately and forwarded to the media pipeline.

This strategy minimizes overlay complexity and serves as a baseline for latency-oriented comparison. Its limitation is scalability in one-to-many scenarios, because the producer must maintain direct outgoing transmission to every consumer.
\subsubsection{Security}
Security is provided by libp2p secure channels and peer identity. Communication is authenticated and encrypted at the transport/session layer (Noise/TLS, depending on transport configuration), and QUIC provides additional modern transport properties useful for real-time traffic. These defaults reduce implementation risk compared to building custom cryptographic channels in application code. \cite{SecurityConsiderations,WhatAreSecure}
\subsubsection{Authentication}
Each node generates a cryptographic peer identity (public/private keypair). This identity is used by libp2p during connection establishment and, in the Gossipsub variant, for signed message authenticity. As a result, receivers can attribute accepted messages to concrete peer identities and apply protocol-level validation decisions.

The current prototype does not implement a full authorization policy (e.g., allowlists, role-based access, signed stream ownership claims). It implements authenticated transport primitives and message validation hooks that can be extended in future iterations.

\section{Current implementation}
The implementation is organized as a Rust workspace consisting of four library crates and two executable binaries. The library crates handle distinct concerns --- screen capture, rendering, \ac{P2P} network transport, and file-based video reading --- while the binaries integrate them for live use and automated testing respectively.

An early prototype used bare MPEG-TS streams, where each MPEG-TS packet has a fixed size of 188 bytes. Forwarding one network packet per MPEG-TS packet produced an extremely high packet rate that saturated the Gossipsub message queue and caused the stream to stall. The fix was to multiplex several MPEG-TS packets into a single larger RTP datagram: \texttt{mpegtsmux} with \texttt{alignment=7} groups seven 188-byte MPEG-TS packets into one 1316-byte RTP payload, reducing the packet rate by a factor of seven. The file-based testing pipeline goes further and adopts direct H.264-in-RTP payloading (\texttt{rtph264pay}) with a configurable MTU of 4096 bytes, eliminating the MPEG-TS layer entirely.

\subsection{Shared data structures and trait interface}
\label{sec:chunk-trait}
All transport variants carry video data as instances of \texttt{VideoStreamChunk}, defined in \texttt{libraries/network/src/protocol.rs}:

\begin{verbatim}
#[derive(Serialize, Deserialize, Debug, Clone)]
pub struct VideoStreamChunk {
    #[serde(with = "serde_bytes")]
    pub chunk: Vec<u8>,
    pub timestamp: SystemTime,
    pub index: u64,
}
\end{verbatim}

The three fields serve distinct purposes. \texttt{chunk} carries the raw RTP payload bytes; the \texttt{serde\_bytes} attribute instructs the CBOR serializer to encode it as a compact byte array rather than as a sequence of integers, which is important for throughput. \texttt{timestamp} records the wall-clock time at which the chunk was created on the producer; the analyzer subtracts this from its local arrival time to derive per-packet latency. \texttt{index} is a monotonically increasing counter used to detect lost packets (gaps in the sequence) and out-of-order delivery (arrivals where \texttt{index} is less than the highest seen so far).

Serialization between peers uses CBOR via the \texttt{serde\_cbor} crate. CBOR is a compact binary encoding that handles raw byte arrays natively and requires no schema, making it straightforward to extend the structure without a versioning step.

The \texttt{P2PSwarm} trait forms the interface boundary between media-handling and transport code:

\begin{verbatim}
#[async_trait]
pub trait P2PSwarm: Send + Sync {
    fn inbound_rx(&self) -> &Receiver<VideoStreamChunk>;
    async fn send_message(&mut self, chunk: VideoStreamChunk);
}
\end{verbatim}

The trait exposes exactly two operations: read access to an \texttt{async\_channel} receiver that delivers inbound chunks, and an async method to enqueue an outbound chunk. Because all media components interact with the network only through this interface, the transport backend can be selected at runtime without any change to capture, rendering, or analysis code.

\subsection{Screen recording crate}
\label{sec:recorder-crate}
Live screen capture is implemented in \texttt{libraries/recorder} using the \texttt{ashpd} crate, which provides a Rust interface to the XDG Desktop Portal screen-cast service. The portal is the standard Wayland mechanism for delegating monitor and window selection and user permission prompts to the compositor; it is supported by all major Wayland compositors including GNOME Mutter and KDE KWin.

The capture initialization sequence is as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \texttt{Screencast::new()} --- opens a D-Bus proxy to the screen-cast portal.
    \item \texttt{create\_session()} --- creates a portal session object.
    \item \texttt{select\_sources()} --- presents a source picker to the user with cursor embedding and support for both monitors and individual application windows.
    \item \texttt{start()} --- begins capture and returns a stream descriptor containing the PipeWire node ID.
    \item \texttt{open\_pipe\_wire\_remote()} --- returns an \texttt{OwnedFd} (PipeWire file descriptor) for the session.
\end{enumerate}

The file descriptor and node ID are passed directly into the GStreamer pipeline.

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1\textwidth]{out/recorder-pipeline/recorder-pipeline.png}
    \caption{Recorder pipeline}
\end{figure}

The GStreamer recorder pipeline elements and their relevant parameters are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{\texttt{pipewiresrc}} with \texttt{fd} and \texttt{path} set to the portal-provided file descriptor and PipeWire node ID --- ingests raw frames from PipeWire at the source capture rate.
    \item \textbf{\texttt{queue leaky=downstream}} --- a bounded queue that discards its oldest pending buffers when the downstream pipeline cannot keep up. Without this element, backpressure from a congested transport layer would propagate upstream and stall the compositor's screen capture.
    \item \textbf{\texttt{videorate}}, \textbf{\texttt{videoconvert}} --- normalize the frame rate to a constant target and convert the pixel format to one acceptable to the encoder.
    \item \textbf{\texttt{timeoverlay}} --- renders a wall-clock timestamp into each frame, useful for visual end-to-end latency verification.
    \item \textbf{H.264 encoding, VA-API path:} \texttt{vaapipostproc} converts the frame to a VA-API surface; \texttt{vaapih264enc quality-level=6 bitrate=1000 keyframe-period=0} encodes it via hardware. A \texttt{keyframe-period} of zero instructs the hardware encoder to insert I-frames adaptively rather than at a fixed interval.
    \item \textbf{H.264 encoding, software fallback:} if VA-API elements are unavailable at runtime, the pipeline substitutes an \texttt{identity} pass-through for the post-processor and \texttt{x264enc} (with \texttt{tune=zerolatency}, \texttt{speed-preset=ultrafast}, \texttt{bitrate=500}, and \texttt{keyframe-period=30}) for the encoder. The \texttt{zerolatency} tune disables B-frame look-ahead and internal output buffering, minimizing encoding latency at some cost to compression efficiency.
    \item \textbf{\texttt{h264parse config-interval=1}} --- instructs the parser to inject SPS and PPS parameter-set NAL units at every key-frame boundary, allowing a newly joining consumer or one recovering from packet loss to resynchronize immediately without waiting for the next natural key-frame.
    \item \textbf{\texttt{mpegtsmux alignment=7}} --- multiplexes the H.264 elementary stream into MPEG-TS and groups seven 188-byte MPEG-TS packets per output buffer, yielding 1316-byte payloads.
    \item \textbf{\texttt{rtpmp2tpay pt=33}} --- wraps each MPEG-TS payload in an RTP datagram using payload type 33, the IANA-registered type for MPEG-TS over RTP.
    \item \textbf{\texttt{appsink max-buffers=1 drop=true}} --- exposes RTP samples to application code. A buffer limit of one combined with \texttt{drop=true} discards any sample that arrives while the previous one has not yet been consumed, preventing unbounded memory growth under transport congestion.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Rendering crate}
\label{sec:renderer-crate}
Incoming RTP H.264 payloads are decoded and displayed by the \texttt{GstRenderer} struct in \texttt{libraries/renderer}. The renderer is built around the RTP H.264 format produced by the file-based test source (\texttt{rtph264pay pt=96}) and processes chunks through the following pipeline:

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1\textwidth]{out/renderer-pipeline/renderer-pipeline.png}
    \caption{Renderer pipeline}
\end{figure}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{\texttt{appsrc}} --- configured with RTP H.264 caps (\texttt{encoding-name=H264}, \texttt{payload=96}, \texttt{clock-rate=90000}), \texttt{is-live=true}, \texttt{do-timestamp=false}, and \texttt{format=Time}. Declaring the caps before the first buffer arrives allows GStreamer to negotiate the downstream pipeline topology immediately.
    \item \textbf{\texttt{rtpjitterbuffer latency=5000}} --- accumulates incoming RTP packets for up to 5000 milliseconds and reorders them by sequence number before passing them downstream. The large window absorbs worst-case reordering from Gossipsub's multi-hop mesh, trading initial latency for stable decoding order.
    \item \textbf{\texttt{rtph264depay}}, \textbf{\texttt{h264parse}} --- strip the RTP framing and reassemble NAL units into a continuous H.264 bytestream.
    \item \textbf{H.264 decoding, fallback chain:} \texttt{vaapih264dec low-latency=true} (hardware, VA-API) is tried first; \texttt{v4l2h264dec} (hardware, V4L2) second; \texttt{avdec\_h264} (software, libavcodec) third. The \texttt{low-latency} property on the VA-API decoder disables its internal frame reordering buffer, reducing decoder-introduced latency.
    \item \textbf{\texttt{vaapipostproc}} (optional) --- inserted when available to perform colorspace conversion on the VA-API surface before the software path.
    \item \textbf{\texttt{videoconvert}}, \textbf{\texttt{queue}}, \textbf{\texttt{autovideosink sync=false}} --- \texttt{sync=false} instructs GStreamer to display each decoded frame immediately upon arrival rather than waiting for the presentation clock. For a live \ac{P2P} stream, minimizing end-to-end latency takes priority over smooth frame pacing.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Transport layer crate}
\label{sec:network-crate}
The \texttt{libraries/network} crate provides two concrete implementations of the \texttt{P2PSwarm} trait. Both share the same underlying stack: QUIC transport over UDP listening on all interfaces at a random ephemeral port (\texttt{/ip4/0.0.0.0/udp/0/quic-v1}), and mDNS for automatic peer discovery on local networks. Each node generates a fresh Ed25519 keypair at startup, giving it a unique but ephemeral cryptographic identity for the duration of the session.

\subsubsection{Gossipsub}
The Gossipsub backend configures strict per-message authentication:

\begin{verbatim}
let gossipsub_config = gossipsub::ConfigBuilder::default()
    .validation_mode(gossipsub::ValidationMode::Strict)
    .validate_messages()
    .build()?;
let gs = gossipsub::Behaviour::new(
    MessageAuthenticity::Signed(local_key.clone()),
    gossipsub_config,
)?;
\end{verbatim}

\texttt{ValidationMode::Strict} combined with \texttt{MessageAuthenticity::Signed} requires every Gossipsub message to carry a valid Ed25519 signature from its originating peer before it is forwarded through the mesh. Each inbound message is validated and the outcome is reported back to the swarm: valid, fresh chunks receive \texttt{MessageAcceptance::Accept} and are forwarded; invalid or stale ones receive \texttt{MessageAcceptance::Reject} and are dropped immediately without further propagation.

A 5-second TTL is applied to every accepted chunk. After CBOR deserialization, the implementation computes the chunk's age and rejects it if too old:

\begin{verbatim}
static TTL: Duration = Duration::from_secs(5);
// ...
let age = SystemTime::now()
    .duration_since(chunk.timestamp)
    .unwrap();
if age > TTL {
    // Reject — do not forward through the mesh
}
\end{verbatim}

This prevents stale video frames from continuing to circulate after their playback window has closed, reducing unnecessary load on the mesh.

Peers are managed through mDNS lifecycle events: on \texttt{mdns::Event::Discovered}, each new peer is dialed and added to the Gossipsub mesh via \texttt{add\_explicit\_peer}; on \texttt{mdns::Event::Expired}, it is removed via \texttt{remove\_explicit\_peer}. A shared, thread-safe peer set (\texttt{HashSet<PeerId>} guarded by an \texttt{Arc<RwLock>}) is kept current on every connection event.

\subsubsection{Stream}
The Stream backend uses \texttt{libp2p\_stream} with a custom versioned protocol identifier:

\begin{verbatim}
const STREAM_PROTOCOL: StreamProtocol
    = StreamProtocol::new("/stream");
\end{verbatim}

On the receiving side, a dedicated Tokio task listens for incoming streams on the \texttt{/stream} protocol, spawning a sub-task per connection. Each sub-task reads the full payload with \texttt{read\_to\_end}, deserializes it from CBOR into a \texttt{VideoStreamChunk}, and forwards the result to the inbound channel.

On the sending side, each \texttt{send\_message} call serializes the chunk into a CBOR byte vector once, then opens a new outbound stream to every peer in the set and writes the same byte vector via \texttt{write\_all}. Serializing once and reusing the byte vector for all destinations avoids redundant encoding work as the consumer count increases.

The same mDNS autodiscovery logic and shared peer set bookkeeping used in the Gossipsub backend are reused unchanged. The Stream backend does not apply a TTL filter; staleness checking is left to the receiving application.

\subsection{Testing binary}
\label{sec:test-binary}
The \texttt{stream\_test} binary is the primary tool for controlled experiments. It accepts two orthogonal command-line arguments via \texttt{clap}:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{\texttt{--protocol}} --- selects the transport backend: \texttt{gossipsub}, \texttt{stream}, or \texttt{hybrid}.
    \item \textbf{\texttt{--role}} --- selects the operational role: \texttt{manager}, \texttt{producer}, \texttt{analyzer}, or \texttt{renderer}.
\end{itemize}

The four roles operate as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Manager} --- spawns two child processes of the same binary: one as \texttt{producer} and one as \texttt{analyzer}, both with the same \texttt{--protocol} flag. It waits for both children to exit or handles Ctrl+C by sending \texttt{SIGINT} to each child before joining them, ensuring clean shutdown of all media pipelines and transport tasks.
    \item \textbf{Producer} --- instantiates a \texttt{VideoReader} (described below) and the chosen transport swarm, then continuously sends every \texttt{VideoStreamChunk} produced by the video pipeline through \texttt{send\_message} until the source reaches end-of-stream.
    \item \textbf{Analyzer} --- subscribes to the inbound channel of the chosen swarm and records each received chunk as an \texttt{AnalysisItem}. After five seconds of inactivity, it writes all collected items to \texttt{Output.csv} using the \texttt{csv} crate:
\begin{verbatim}
#[derive(Serialize)]
pub struct AnalysisItem {
    creation_time: u128,
    arrival_time: u128,
    index: u64,
    size: u32,
}
\end{verbatim}
    \texttt{creation\_time} and \texttt{arrival\_time} are UNIX timestamps in milliseconds; their difference gives per-packet one-way latency. \texttt{index} allows detection of lost and out-of-order packets. \texttt{size} is the chunk payload length plus 24 bytes for the serialized \texttt{timestamp} and \texttt{index} metadata.
    \item \textbf{Renderer} --- receives inbound chunks and pushes each raw byte payload into \texttt{GstRenderer}, producing a live decoded video window for visual inspection.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Video reader pipeline}
\label{sec:video-reader-pipeline}
The \texttt{VideoReader} in \texttt{libraries/video-reader} reads a pre-recorded MP4 file specified by the \texttt{VIDEO\_SOURCE} environment variable and emits it as a sequence of RTP H.264 \texttt{VideoStreamChunk} values at the file's original encoded frame rate. Its GStreamer pipeline is designed to produce output in the same RTP H.264 format as a live capture session, keeping test results directly comparable. The pipeline stages are:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \texttt{filesrc} $\rightarrow$ \texttt{qtdemux} --- reads and demultiplexes the MP4 container; the H.264 elementary stream is exposed on a dynamic pad.
    \item \texttt{h264parse} --- normalizes the H.264 bytestream.
    \item \textbf{Decode:} \texttt{vaapih264dec} (hardware, VA-API) with fallback to \texttt{avdec\_h264} (software). Converting to raw frames is required for the subsequent re-encoding step.
    \item \texttt{timeoverlay} --- embeds a wall-clock timestamp for visual end-to-end latency verification.
    \item \textbf{Re-encode:} \texttt{vaapih264enc quality-level=6 bitrate=500} with fallback to \texttt{x264enc tune=zerolatency speed-preset=ultrafast bitrate=500 keyframe-period=30}. Re-encoding produces a fresh H.264 stream with the same encoder settings as the live recorder, ensuring format and bitrate compatibility across test scenarios.
    \item \texttt{h264parse config-interval=1} --- injects inline SPS/PPS parameter sets.
    \item \texttt{rtph264pay mtu=4096 pt=96} --- packetizes NAL units into RTP datagrams with payload type 96 and an MTU of $2^{12}$ = 4096 bytes. The large MTU produces substantially fewer packets per second than the 1316-byte MPEG-TS payloads of the recorder, reducing serialization and dispatch overhead in the transport layer.
    \item \texttt{appsink max-buffers=1 drop=true} --- exposes samples to application code with the same back-pressure policy as the recorder.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Testing of stability and performance}
\subsection{Reusable source}
To facilitate consistent and repeatable testing, I made an implementation of video source, that uses a pre-recorded video file as the source of video data instead of live screen capture. This allows me to have a controlled environment for testing, where the input video stream remains constant across different test runs. This is crucial for accurately measuring performance metrics such as latency, packet loss, and throughput, as it eliminates variability that could arise from changing screen content during live capture.

I have decided to use Big Buck Bunny video as the source video for testing. Big Buck Bunny is commonly used for testing and benchmarking video processing applications because it is royalty-free and open-source. \cite{BigBuckBunny}
\subsection{Testing metadata}
To effectively monitor and analyze the performance of the \ac{P2P} video streaming application, I have added metadata to each video packet being transmitted. This metadata includes:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Packet creation timestamp - This allows me to measure the time taken for each packet to travel from the sender to the receiver
    \item Packet sequence number - This helps in identifying lost or out-of-order packets during transmission
\end{itemize}
\subsection{Latency testing}
To measure the latency of the video streaming application, I calculate the time difference between when a packet is created (using the creation timestamp) and when it is received by the consumer. By averaging these time differences over multiple packets, I can obtain an overall latency metric for the streaming session. This metric is crucial for assessing the responsiveness of the \ac{P2P} video streaming application, as high latency can lead to noticeable delays in video playback and negatively impact the user experience.
\subsection{Packet loss testing}
Packet loss is determined by missing sequence numbers in the received packets. By tracking the sequence numbers of incoming packets, I can identify any gaps that indicate lost packets during transmission. The packet loss rate is calculated as the ratio of lost packets to the total number of packets sent. This metric is important for evaluating the reliability of the \ac{P2P} video streaming application, as high packet loss can lead to degraded video quality and interruptions in playback.
\subsection{Packet order variability}
To evaluate the order in which packets are received, I analyze the sequence numbers of incoming packets. By tracking the sequence numbers, I can determine if packets are arriving in the correct order or if there are instances of out-of-order delivery. This metric influences additional delay for reordering packets before decoding and rendering, which heavily impacts stream latency.
\subsection{Late packet testing}
Late packets are identified by comparing the arrival time of each packet against a predefined threshold based on the expected playback time. If a packet arrives after its scheduled playback time, it is considered late. Late packets are discarded as they cannot be used for real-time playback.
The late packet rate is calculated as the ratio of late packets to the total number of packets received. This metric is important for understanding how well the streaming application can maintain smooth playback, as late packets can lead to buffering and interruptions in the video stream.
\subsection{Throughput testing}
Throughput is measured by calculating the total amount of data successfully received over a specific time interval. By tracking the size of incoming packets and the time taken to receive them, I can compute the average data rate in bits per second (bps). This metric provides insight into the efficiency of the \ac{P2P} video streaming application in utilizing available network bandwidth.
I can achieve throughput measurment by testing on different source video bitrates and seeing change in other metrics like latency and packet loss.
This will categorize the testing into multiple bitrates, comparing how well the transport layer handles different levels of data load.

\subsection{Hybrid Transport Architectures}
Subsequent research could explore more complex transport layer implementations that combine the benefits of both Gossipsub and stream protocols. The gossipsub could be used as signaling layer to discover video chunks on the network, while stream protocol could be used for direct peer-to-peer transmission of video data. This hybrid approach could potentially provide the scalability and resilience of Gossipsub while also achieving the low latency and reliable delivery of stream protocol.
\subsection{Large scale stress testing}
To validate the system's robustness, future studies must prioritize large-scale stress testing of the \ac{P2P} application. This involves deploying the protocol across a high volume of geographically distributed peers in a real-world network environment. The primary objective is to evaluate performance metrics—specifically jitter, latency, and packet loss—under high-concurrency conditions.
